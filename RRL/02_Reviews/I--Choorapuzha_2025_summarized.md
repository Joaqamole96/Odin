## TL;DR

Self-employed musicians can achieve financial stability through cash-flow tracking, a two-bank salary system, a 6-to-12-month emergency fund, and prioritized retirement savings.

## Problem and Motivation

Self-employed musicians lack employer-provided benefits such as steady income, tax withholding, insurance, and retirement plans, creating unique financial challenges. This financial insecurity causes many musicians to leave the profession, harming the sustainability of classical music careers. Prior advice for musicians was fragmented and lacked a structured, sequential roadmap tailored to irregular income.

## Approach

- Track all monthly income and expenses using free apps or spreadsheets to give every dollar a purpose.
- Apply the 50/30/20 rule: 50% of income to needs, 30% to wants, 20% to savings.
- Calculate monthly overhead and required revenue to decide when to take or decline work.
- Use two bank accounts: deposit all income into Bank A, then pay yourself a fixed bi-weekly salary into Bank B.
- Build an emergency fund covering 6–12 months of living expenses in a high-yield savings account.
- Save separately for insurance deductibles (auto, home, health, instrument) as a second-layer reserve.
- Pay off high-interest debt (interest >5%), prioritizing either smallest balances for emotional wins or highest rates for financial efficiency.
- Max out retirement contributions annually using a Roth or Traditional IRA; consider an HSA if eligible.
- Invest in low-cost, broad-based index funds (e.g., VTSAX at 0.04% fee) and avoid high-fee active managers.

## Findings

- Budgeting transforms dollars from scattered earnings into intentional tools, giving the spender control *("your dollars are your orchestra and you are the conductor")*.
- The two-bank salary system stabilizes irregular cash flow, enabling consistent bi-weekly pay regardless of actual income timing.
- **A $24,000–$48,000 emergency fund** (6–12 months of expenses for a $4,000 monthly budget) protects against injury or income loss without incurring debt.
- At 5% interest, a $24,000 emergency fund grows to nearly $40,000 after ten years through compounding.
- Over the last 30 years, the US total stock market (VTSAX) returned over 10% annually with a 0.04% fee.
- An HSA offers a triple tax advantage: deductible contributions, tax-free growth, and tax-free withdrawals for medical expenses.
- Paying off high-interest debt above 5% is mathematically superior to keeping it if investments earn less than that rate.

## Definitions

| Term / Acronym | Plain-English Definition |
| -------------- | ------------------------ |
| IRA | Individual Retirement Account – a tax-advantaged retirement savings account you open yourself. |
| Roth IRA | Retirement account where you pay taxes now, then withdraw both contributions and growth tax-free in retirement. |
| Traditional IRA | Retirement account where you deduct contributions now, but pay taxes on withdrawals later. |
| HSA | Health Savings Account – a tax-advantaged account for medical expenses, usable as an additional retirement vehicle. [Think: triple tax benefit – in, growth, out all tax-free for medical costs.] |
| High-yield savings account | An online savings account paying higher interest (currently ~5%) than regular bank savings accounts (0.01%). |
| Deductible | The amount you must pay out-of-pocket before insurance covers the rest of a claim. |

## Critical Citations

- [Ramsey, 2009] – Popularized the $1,000 emergency fund, which the author rejects as too low for musicians.
- [Collins, 2016] – Argues for simple, low-cost index fund investing (VTSAX) as the path to wealth.
- [Bogle, 2017] – Founder of Vanguard; foundational text on the superiority of low-cost passive index funds.
- [Stanley & Danko, 2010] – Established that wealth comes from budgeting and intentional spending, not high income.
