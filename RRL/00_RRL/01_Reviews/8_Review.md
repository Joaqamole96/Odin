# **Anomaly Detection in Personal Finance Systems**

The unsupervised nature of anomaly detection in personal finance management systems imposes fundamental design constraints that differentiate it from supervised fraud detection in banking. D'Souza et al. (2026) establish that anomaly detection in PFMS is inherently unsupervised, with Isolation Forest highlighted as superior to density-based methods for scalable deviation detection in transaction streams without labeled training data. Harris and Austin (2024) similarly argue that unsupervised learning detects anomalies without labeled data, making it applicable to cold-start user profiles where no prior examples of anomalous spending exist. Machireddy (2023) demonstrates that unsupervised autoencoders detect spending anomalies via reconstruction error, solving the cold-start detection problem directly, while Gouni (2025) confirms that autoencoders capture non-linear relationships that linear methods miss, requiring no labeled fraud data. Brophy et al. (2023) extend this to time-series GANs, showing that unsupervised GANs (MAD-GAN) detect anomalies in multivariate time series without labels, though training instability and lack of standardized evaluation metrics remain open challenges. Begum (2025) provides a critical caveat: Isolation Forest precision is only 0.65 on imbalanced financial data, making it unsuitable as a standalone detector but useful as a supplementary method within a hybrid pipeline. This limitation is compounded by concept drift: as user financial behavior evolves due to job changes, income shifts, or seasonal obligations, static anomaly detectors increase false positives over time. Xiang et al. (2023) and Liao (2026) formalize that concept drift adaptation — distinguishing abrupt, gradual, and recurring drift — is necessary to maintain detection accuracy, with parameter updates (weight adjustments) converging faster than full structure updates for mobile deployment. For Odin, these findings justify a hybrid anomaly detection architecture: Isolation Forest or autoencoders for cold-start unsupervised detection, supplemented by user feedback loops and periodic retraining to adapt to behavioral drift, with drift detection mechanisms (e.g., ADWIN or STEPD) triggering model updates when spending patterns shift significantly.

Comparative benchmarking across algorithm families reveals trade-offs between accuracy, false positive rates, and computational suitability for mobile deployment. Polytarchos (2025) directly compares batch-trained deep learning against real-time unsupervised clustering on credit card transaction data: LSTM achieved 92% accuracy predicting customer total funds range, while real-time clustering (BEReTiC) achieved a substantially lower 0.003% misclassification rate (574 false positives) compared to deep learning's 0.007% rate (1,340 false positives), though clustering's per-label accuracy reached only 66% during cold-start. Begum (2025) reports that a stacking ensemble of XGBoost, Random Forest, and GRU achieves F1 = 0.89 and precision = 0.91 for sequence-based anomaly detection, outperforming any single classifier, while Bader and Haraty (2025) document that Isolation Forest and One-Class SVM achieve 90% precision and 0.93 ROC-AUC on financial transactions, with real-time anomaly alerts and user feedback loops reducing false positives over time. Zlobin and Bazylevych (2025) provide the strongest benchmark from a systematic review: random forest with ensemble feature selection achieved 99.6% accuracy and 100% precision in credit card fraud detection (F1 99.6%), while XGBoost consistently outperformed all deep learning models across 10 credit scoring datasets on AUC, Brier score, and expected maximum profit, being computationally cheaper. However, Scrivano (2025) shows that Transformer models achieved 12–17% lower MAE than LSTM on retail time-series forecasting, and Temporal Convolutional Networks attained 20% higher accuracy in early-stage anomaly detection from sensor data, suggesting that architecture choice depends on data characteristics and computational constraints. Bhavana et al. (2025) report that anomaly detection using Isolation Forest and autoencoders achieved 95% accuracy in a pilot study, with SHAP and LIME explainability translating anomaly scores into natural language (e.g., "grocery spending 38% higher due to festival"), which improved user trust for 78% of participants. Alammar et al. (2026) provide a regulatory-grade template: a hybrid SHAP–LIME framework with Bayesian uncertainty reduced fairness gaps by 40% while maintaining near-optimal utility (0.981 ± 0.008), with explanation consistency tracked over time reaching 0.967. For Odin's mobile-first, manual-input architecture, these findings support a tiered strategy: lightweight tree-based ensembles (XGBoost or random forest) for behavioral profile classification and rule-based anomaly thresholds during cold-start, transitioning to Isolation Forest or autoencoders as transaction history accumulates, with LSTM or GRU reserved for server-side processing of sequential patterns when sufficient data exists, and SHAP/LIME explanations embedded in alert interfaces to justify why a transaction was flagged as anomalous.

## Synthesis

The methodological range of reviewed works spans systematic reviews of deep learning for anomaly detection (Zlobin & Bazylevych, Xiang et al.), comparative empirical studies of unsupervised versus supervised methods (Polytarchos, Begum, D'Souza et al.), domain-agnostic surveys of time-series anomaly detection (Brophy et al., Gouni, Scrivano), and applied XAI frameworks for financial systems (Alammar et al., Bhavana et al., Machireddy). The convergent finding across this methodological diversity is that no single algorithm dominates anomaly detection in personal finance; rather, design choices must balance unsupervised cold-start capability, false positive rates, adaptation to concept drift, and computational constraints of mobile deployment. Unsupervised methods — Isolation Forest, autoencoders, and clustering — are essential for new users with no transaction history, while ensemble methods (XGBoost, random forest) and recurrent architectures (LSTM, GRU) achieve higher accuracy when sufficient labeled or sequential data exists. For Odin, this convergence directly justifies a modular anomaly detection pipeline: an unsupervised filter (Isolation Forest or autoencoder) operating on-device for real-time flagging of spending deviations from learned patterns, a concept drift monitor (using statistical process control or window-based methods) to detect when legitimate user behavior changes and trigger model updates, and an explainability layer (SHAP or LIME) to generate user-facing justifications for each anomaly alert, addressing the trust requirements documented in Filipino young professionals' sensitivity to financial advice transparency.

Several critical gaps emerge from the literature that directly motivate Odin's anomaly detection design. First, no existing study evaluates anomaly detection algorithms on manual-entry personal finance data from Filipino young professionals; all benchmarks use credit card fraud datasets (European, US, Greek) or synthetic transaction streams that lack culturally specific spending patterns such as remittance obligations, paluwagan cycles, and petsa de peligro spending spikes. Second, concept drift adaptation methods are validated on electricity, weather, or network intrusion data — not on personal spending sequences where drift is driven by payday cycles, 13th month pay, and seasonal family obligations — leaving open how drift detection thresholds should be calibrated for Filipino income volatility. Third, the trade-off between detection accuracy and mobile computational constraints is rarely quantified: reported performance metrics (e.g., 99.6% fraud detection accuracy) assume server-grade hardware, not the battery-limited, offline-capable environment of a mobile-first PFMS. Odin addresses these gaps by designing category-specific anomaly thresholds that treat family remittances and emergency obligations as protected spending categories, reducing false positives for culturally necessary expenses; by implementing lightweight drift detection (e.g., monitoring rolling means and standard deviations of per-category spending) that does not require full model retraining; and by prioritizing ensemble methods (XGBoost, random forest) over deep learning for on-device inference, reserving LSTM or Transformer processing for optional server-side batch analysis when users opt into cloud synchronization under RA 10173 privacy safeguards.
