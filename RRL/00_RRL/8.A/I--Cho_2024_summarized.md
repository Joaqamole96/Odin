## TL;DR

Ethnic minority young adults in Hong Kong employ diverse budgeting and saving strategies, with enabling factors including family social capital and intrapersonal characteristics shaping their financial decisions.

## Problem and Motivation

Understanding of financial decision-making among racial/ethnic minority young adults is limited to Western contexts, quantitative methods, and narrow domains like banking and retirement, excluding day-to-day decisions. This gap matters because younger minorities face double vulnerabilities (ethnicity and age) that affect their financial well-being. Prior work had not qualitatively examined a broad range of financial decisions (budgeting, spending, planning, product use, debt, fraud detection) in a non-Western context like Hong Kong.

## Approach

- Semi-structured individual interviews with 53 Pakistani, Indian, Nepalese, and Filipino young adults aged 18–29 in Hong Kong.
- Purposive sampling through NGOs and university international offices; 30 males, 23 females; 32 employed, 21 students.
- Thematic analysis of six financial decision domains: personal budgeting, spending, financial planning, use of financial products, debt management, and fraud detection.
- Explored both practices/strategies and perceptions; interviews conducted in English, lasting 60–75 minutes.
- Identified enabling factors: family social capital, intrapersonal characteristics, social dynamics, knowledge proficiency, and facilitative contextual circumstances.

## Findings

- **Many saved approximately one-third of their income** using tactics like parental monitoring, separate bank accounts, and stashing cash.
- Budgeting strategies included digital tools (apps, spreadsheets), parental monitoring, and mental bucketing; challenges included limited funds, self-control, and stress.
- Spending philosophies varied: needs-vs-wants, work incentive, and YOLO (You Only Live Once).
- Informal borrowing from family and friends was common; one-third owned credit cards, with occasional risks from minimum payments.
- Fraud victimization occurred despite protective tactics (ignoring suspicious calls, pretending not to speak Cantonese).
- Enabling factors identified: family social capital (parental role modeling, financial coaching, sibling support), intrapersonal characteristics (self-discipline, self-motivation, future orientation), peer support and vigilance, knowledge of financial concepts and anti-fraud measures, and contextual circumstances (employment, pandemic lessons, technology access).

## Critical Citations

- [Lusardi & Mitchell, 2007] — Foundational evidence that financial literacy is associated with retirement planning and asset holding.
- [Atkinson & Messy, 2012] — Defines financial literacy as combination of awareness, knowledge, skill, attitude, and behavior for sound financial decisions.
- [Thaler, 1985] — Introduces mental accounting theory, explaining why users violate fungibility (e.g., treating credit cards as separate mental accounts).
- [Danes, 1994] — Establishes financial socialization theory, which this paper extends by showing persistent parental influence into young adulthood for ethnic minorities.

**Contribution to Odin:**

This qualitative study informs Odin's understanding of how young adults actually budget, spend, save, and detect fraud outside Western contexts. The enabling factors framework directly supports Odin's behavioral profiling module by identifying intrapersonal characteristics (self-discipline, motivation, future orientation) as key differentiators. The finding that budgeting is challenging due to self-control and time demands justifies Odin's design priority of reducing entry friction and making budgeting effortless through digital tools. Although the study focuses on Hong Kong ethnic minorities, the observed patterns of informal borrowing, family financial monitoring, and fraud vulnerability are transferable to Filipino young professionals.

**Directly justifies:**

- "Many ethnic minority young adults save approximately one-third of their income, indicating a strong baseline saving habit that budget recommendation systems can reinforce."
- "Informal borrowing from family and friends is common and carries social shame; personal finance systems should accommodate intra-family transfers without penalizing users."
- "Budgeting challenges include limited funds, self-control, and perceived time demands — features that reduce entry friction (e.g., automated tracking, low-effort inputs) are essential for sustained engagement."
- "Fraud victimization is prevalent among young adults who employ only passive protective tactics (ignoring suspicious calls); anomaly detection modules must be proactive and incorporate scam pattern recognition."
- "Intrapersonal characteristics (self-discipline, self-motivation, future orientation) shape financial decision-making outcomes and should be incorporated into user behavioral profiles."

**Limits of relevance:**

- Study population is ethnic minorities in Hong Kong, not Filipino young professionals in Metro Manila; cultural and economic contexts differ (e.g., cost of living, social safety nets).
- Qualitative design with small sample (n=53) and well-educated, English-speaking participants; findings may not generalize to lower-SES populations.
- No algorithmic or quantitative evaluation; provides behavioral insights but no performance metrics for classification, forecasting, or anomaly detection.
- Focuses on individual decision-making, not system design or evaluation; does not address mobile-first constraints, privacy, or ISO 25010 evaluation.

## Limitations

- Sample was generally well educated (half had bachelor’s degrees) and English-proficient; may not reflect lower socio-economic status ethnic minorities.
- Interviews conducted in English, excluding participants with limited English command.
- Qualitative study with small sample size (n=53) limits generalizability; quantitative replication needed.
- No comparison group of ethnic Chinese young adults; differences and similarities not examined. [unacknowledged]
- Does not evaluate the effectiveness of any financial education or system intervention; purely descriptive.
