## TL;DR

Windfall gains increase preference for hedonic spending, but a high scarcity mindset suppresses this effect; hard-earned money consistently favors utilitarian spending.

## Problem and Motivation

Mental accounting theory predicts that windfall gains (“happy money”) drive hedonic consumption, while hard-earned money drives utilitarian consumption. It is unclear whether a scarcity mindset—the belief that resources are limited—moderates this relationship. No prior study had tested the asymmetric moderating role of scarcity mindset across both mental account types.

## Approach

- Two online experiments: student sample (n=319, mean age 19.8) and adult sample (n=294, mean age 48.1) in China.
- 2 (mental account: windfall vs. hard-earning) × 2 (consumption type: hedonic vs. utilitarian) between-subjects design.
- Scarcity mindset measured with 3-item scale (α=0.88 student, 0.83 adult).
- Students chose: deposit 100 RMB to canteen card (utilitarian) vs. dinner at high-quality restaurant (hedonic). Adults chose: buy large home appliance (utilitarian) vs. travel/booking hotel (hedonic).
- Logistic regression tested moderation.

## Findings

1. Across both samples, windfall gains led to **higher hedonic choice** (students: 33.45 χ², d=0.68, p<0.001; adults: 10.30 χ², d=0.38, p=0.001).
2. Scarcity mindset **reduced hedonic preference under windfalls** (students: B=-0.66, p=0.026; adults: B=-1.28, p<0.001).
3. Under hard-earned money, scarcity mindset had **no significant effect** on consumption choices.

- Adult sample showed weaker overall hedonic preference than students, possibly due to larger windfall amount (3,000 RMB vs. 100 RMB) and greater financial responsibility.

## Definitions

| Term / Acronym | Plain-English Definition |
| -------------- | ------------------------ |
| Mental accounting | Cognitive operations where people label money by its source and intended use, treating it as non-fungible. |
| Scarcity mindset | Belief that essential resources (money, food, time) are limited and one cannot have enough. |
| Hedonic consumption | Spending focused on pleasure, enjoyment, and experiential aspects (e.g., dining out, travel). |
| Utilitarian consumption | Spending focused on practical, necessary, functional items (e.g., food staples, appliances). |
| Windfall gains | Unexpected or easy-come money (lottery winnings, gifts) – “happy money.” |

## Critical Citations

**Directly justifies:**

- “A high scarcity mindset eliminates the windfall-to-hedonic effect, making users spend windfalls as cautiously as hard-earned money.”
- “Scarcity mindset moderates mental accounting asymmetrically: it suppresses hedonic consumption under windfalls but does not alter utilitarian consumption under hard-earned income.”

**Limits of relevance:**

- Participants are Chinese; cultural attitudes toward thrift and indulgence may differ from Filipino young professionals.
- Scarcity mindset measured as a trait, not experimentally induced; causality direction is correlational.
- Windfall amounts (100 RMB student, 3,000 RMB adult) may not map to typical Filipino income or bonus sizes.
- No validation of hedonic/utilitarian product choices for Filipino cultural categories (e.g., “travel” may be seen as utilitarian for OFW families).

## Limitations

- Self-reported scarcity mindset scale lacks validity testing beyond reliability; may conflate trait and state scarcity.
- No manipulation check for mental accounting assignment (assumed random assignment valid). [unacknowledged]
- Adult sample recruited via social media (WeChat), potentially biasing toward higher digital literacy.
- Study does not control for income level, debt, or family size, which could interact with scarcity mindset.
- Hedonic/utilitarian stimuli not validated for cultural relevance outside China; “canteen card” has no direct Filipino equivalent.
