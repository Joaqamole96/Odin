## TL;DR

Platform work in ASEAN systematically excludes workers from social protection by design, requiring portable benefit models that delink coverage from employment status.

## Problem and Motivation

Platform workers across ASEAN are legally misclassified as independent contractors rather than employees, severing their access to nationally mandated social security schemes. This exclusion creates a growing precariat facing health, injury, and old-age risks without safety nets, undermining social equity in the region's digital transformation. Prior work lacked a systematic, cross-country analysis of protection gaps specific to ASEAN's gig economy and did not propose delinked, portable benefit mechanisms.

## Approach

- Conceptual-legal analysis of platform business models and worker classification across ASEAN (Indonesia, Thailand, Malaysia, Philippines).
- Identification of a tripartite crisis: coverage, financing, and portability of social protection.
- Typology of protection gaps via a comparative table mapping health, pension, income security, and collective voice.
- Mapping of regulatory responses: proactive (Thailand judicial rulings, Malaysia SKSPS) vs. laissez-faire (Indonesia, Philippines).
- Policy pathway generation: portable benefit accounts, transaction levies, and earning-based contributions.

## Findings

- Platform work's legal design as technology intermediary denies employer status, legally excluding workers from employment-linked social security.
- Three interdependent gaps exist: health/work injury (no workers' compensation), pension/old-age (no employer contributions), income security (no unemployment protection), and collective voice (unions barred).
- Thailand's Social Security Court has ruled some platform workers as employees; Malaysia's SKSPS offers state-subsidized voluntary gig worker coverage — pioneering but limited.
- Platforms offer fragmented, non-portable insurance products in Indonesia and Philippines, with low uptake due to cost and awareness.
- **The current trajectory entrenches a digitally-enabled precariat** unless protection is delinked from employment contracts.
