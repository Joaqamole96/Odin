## TL;DR

Financial literacy training framed as care for indebted Filipino migrant workers in Dubai individualizes debt, obscuring structural drivers such as the kafala system, family obligations, and exclusionary banking regulations.

## Problem and Motivation

State and private initiatives promote financial literacy training to turn indebted overseas Filipino workers (OFWs) into debt-free future entrepreneurs, but these programs bypass the political-economic conditions that produce migrant debt. Understanding how debt shapes OFWs’ daily lives and how care practices operate within such training is missing from migration and debt scholarship. Prior work had not critically examined the “conflicted care” embedded in financial literacy education targeting migrants.

## Approach

- Qualitative study based on 30 semi-structured interviews with Filipino migrant workers in Dubai and Abu Dhabi, conducted in 2018.
- Focus on IGNITE, a civil society organization offering six-month entrepreneurship and financial literacy training to OFWs.
- Analyzed IGNITE’s training content, volunteer perspectives, and participant experiences of debt and financial management.
- Examined how financial literacy is framed as care through values of sacrifice, delayed gratification, and curbing “conspicuous consumption.”

## Findings

- IGNITE’s training promotes financial “maturity” by urging OFWs to reduce remittances to families and cut spending on leisure and self-care, framing debt as a personal moral failure.
- Participants reported that structural factors—rising living costs in Dubai, family emergencies in the Philippines, and lack of affordable housing—drive debt far more than individual overspending.
- Migration-related debts (airfare, visa costs, pawning land) and family debts (housing repairs, land rights) are common but ignored in financial literacy curricula.
- UAE banking regulations exclude lower-income OFWs from formal credit, pushing them to loan sharks or employers, which binds them further to precarious jobs.
- Unpaid debts can lead to passport confiscation, travel bans, deportation, or imprisonment, yet IGNITE and Philippine government agents dismiss such cases as individual irresponsibility.

## Definitions

| -------------- | ------------------------ |
| OFW | Overseas Filipino Worker — a Filipino citizen working abroad. |
| kafala | UAE’s sponsorship system tying a migrant’s legal status to a specific employer. [think: employer as legal gatekeeper] |
| balikbayan box | A box of goods sent by OFWs to family in the Philippines as a care package. |
| IGNITE | Pseudonym for the civil society organization studied; provides financial literacy and entrepreneurship training. |
| Emiratization | UAE policy prioritizing Emirati citizens for jobs, reducing foreign workers’ job security. |

**Contribution to Odin:**

This paper directly informs Odin’s understanding of its target demographic by showing that Filipino young professionals’ financial behavior is shaped by structural obligations—remittances, family debt, and migration costs—not just individual spending choices. The critique of financial literacy training’s moralizing tone warns Odin against designing budget recommendation or anomaly detection modules that blame users for debt without accounting for culturally specific pressures (e.g., emergency family support, balikbayan box expenses). The finding that debt leads to shame and concealment also supports Odin’s privacy-by-design and non-judgmental alert design.

**Directly justifies:**

- “Filipino migrant workers incur debt to finance migration (airfare, visa costs, pawning assets) and to support family emergencies, not primarily from ‘conspicuous consumption’.”
- “Financial literacy training that emphasizes delayed gratification and individual sacrifice obscures structural drivers of debt such as the kafala system and exclusionary banking regulations.”
- “Debt-related shame and fear of legal consequences (passport confiscation, imprisonment) lead migrants to conceal financial distress, which has implications for user trust in personal finance systems.”
- “Family obligations in the Philippines—including housing repairs, medical bills, and land rights payments—constitute non-discretionary expenses that personal finance systems must accommodate as protected categories.”

**Limits of relevance:**

- Study focuses on OFWs in Dubai under the kafala system; Filipino young professionals in Metro Manila face different labor and migration contexts.
- Qualitative sample (n=30) provides rich narratives but no generalizable quantitative claims about debt prevalence.
- The critique of financial literacy is not directly transferable to algorithmic budget recommendation, but the underlying principle (avoid moralizing debt) applies to user interface and alert design.
- Paper does not evaluate any personal finance management system, only a training organization.

## Limitations

- Study limited to OFWs in Dubai and Abu Dhabi; findings may not generalize to non-migrant Filipino young professionals in Metro Manila.
- No quantitative data on debt levels or financial literacy outcomes; claims about debt drivers are qualitative and contextual.
- [unacknowledged] The paper does not compare IGNITE’s training to other financial interventions, so the degree to which alternative approaches might be more effective remains unknown.
- [unacknowledged] Potential researcher bias: the author’s critical stance on financial literacy may overstate its failures while underreporting participant benefits mentioned in passing.
- Sample includes mostly professional and skilled workers; domestic workers (a more vulnerable group) are underrepresented, with only one cited.

## Remember This

- 🔑 OFW debt is structural — migration costs, family emergencies, and banking exclusion drive it, not “luxury spending.”
- 📌 Financial literacy training moralizes debt as personal failure — Odin must avoid this tone in alerts and recommendations.
- 💡 Family obligations in the Philippines (housing, medical, land rights) are non-negotiable expenses — treat them as protected categories.
- ⚠️ Debt leads to shame and concealment — Odin’s privacy design must reduce, not increase, user fear of judgment.
- 🧠 No quantitative benchmark — but the qualitative insight that debt is relational (family, employer, loan shark) is critical for profile classification.
