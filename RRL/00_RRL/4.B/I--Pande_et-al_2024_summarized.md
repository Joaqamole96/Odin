## TL;DR

Personal financial planning research grew sharply after 2010, with behavioral finance, digital tools, and financial literacy as dominant themes across 158 Scopus articles from 2004–2024.

## Problem and Motivation

Despite growing literature on personal financial planning, no comprehensive systematic review had mapped two decades of research trends, key journals, institutional contributions, and geographic distributions. Understanding these patterns is essential for identifying gaps and guiding future research. Existing narrative reviews lacked the structured search and filtering rigor of PRISMA-based systematic reviews.

## Approach

- Systematic Literature Review (SLR) using the PRISMA framework
- Database: Scopus; search period 2004–2024
- Keywords: “Personal Financial Planning”, “Personal Wealth Management”, “Financial Wellness”, “Personal Financial Management”
- Inclusion filters: subject areas (Economics, Finance, Business, Social Sciences); document types (articles, conference papers, reviews, editorials); final stage; English language
- Final corpus: 158 documents after exclusion of 191 records (duplicates, period, subject area, document type, publication stage, source type, language)
- Content analysis: yearly publication trends, most productive journals, affiliations, country distributions

## Findings

1. Publications averaged <5 per year from 2004–2009, then rose sharply after 2010 with peaks in 2017, 2019, 2020, and 2023.
2. **Journal of Financial Counselling and Planning** produced the most documents (17), followed by Family and Consumer Sciences Research Journal (7).
3. Kansas State University led institutional output (9 papers), then Ohio State University (8).
4. United States contributed 62 publications (39% of total); India (19), Malaysia (18), South Africa (10), Canada (9) followed.

- Behavioral finance, digital financial planning tools, and financial literacy are the three dominant recurring themes.
- Financial technology (FinTech) and robo-advisors have democratized access to financial services and improved financial literacy.
- Gaps remain in debt literacy, digital divide, and cross-cultural comparative studies.

## Limitations

- Literature search limited to Scopus database; may have missed relevant publications indexed elsewhere.
- Possible bias in paper selection and result interpretation acknowledged by authors.
- No quantitative meta-analysis — trends are descriptive only.
- Excludes non-English papers, potentially missing region-specific insights from Asia and Latin America. [unacknowledged]
- Does not assess quality or methodological rigor of the reviewed studies — only presence/absence of themes.
