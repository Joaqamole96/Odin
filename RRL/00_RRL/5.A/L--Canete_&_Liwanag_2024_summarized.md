## TL;DR

Ubos-biyaya (lavish spending) and petsa de peligro (pre-payday financial strain) are phenomenologically linked: capitalism's planned obsolescence conditions ubos-biyaya, which then produces petsa de peligro.

## Problem and Motivation

The paper addresses the gap in understanding the relationship between Filipino spending behaviors (ubos-biyaya) and financial precarity (petsa de peligro) within a capitalist system. It matters because these behaviors perpetuate economic exclusion and resource depletion, yet are often dismissed as mere impulsivity rather than systemic outcomes. Prior work lacked a phenomenological integration linking consumer culture, planned obsolescence, and the cyclical nature of Filipino financial distress.

## Approach

- Phenomenological reflection on Filipino terms ubos-biyaya (depleting resources) and petsa de peligro (danger day before payday).
- Conceptual analysis linking ubos-biyaya to planned obsolescence and "cathedrals of consumption" (Ewen, Ritzer).
- Draws on Church documents (Evangelii Gaudium, Centesimus Annus, Laudato Si) to critique capitalism's economy of exclusion.
- Uses unpublished interviews (e.g., Amy Cundangan) and online articles to illustrate Filipino impulsive buying behavior.
- Advocates for an economy of inclusion grounded in solidarity and the common good as an alternative framework.

## Findings

- Ubos-biyaya is not mere impulsive buying but a **preconditioned outcome** of capitalism's planned obsolescence and cathedral-of-consumption design.
- Petsa de peligro emerges as an upshot of ubos-biyaya: the period when money runs low and payday feels far away.
- Capitalism creates a "throwaway culture" (Pope Francis) where persons are reduced to commodities and the poor are excluded from participation.
- 87% of Filipinos buy more than planned when shopping, compared to 75% of Americans (Lagman & Ocampo, 2017).
- The Church proposes an economy of inclusion based on solidarity, prioritizing life over appropriation of goods by the few.

## Definitions

| Term / Acronym | Plain-English Definition |
| -------------- | ------------------------ |
| Ubos-biyaya | Filipino term for spending all resources (tangible and intangible) impulsively on pseudo-needs. |
| Petsa de peligro | Filipino term for the "day of danger" — the period before payday when financial constraints intensify. |
| Pseudo-needs | Illusory desires that feel like necessities but are actually tertiary wants (Bukatman). |
| Planned obsolescence | Designing products with a deliberately short useful life to force repeat consumption. |
| Cathedral of consumption | Any sphere (e.g., malls) where trade and consumption are regulated to control and exploit consumers (Weber/Ritzer). |
| Economy of exclusion | A capitalist system that reduces goods and persons to commodities, discarding those who cannot generate profit (Pope Francis). |
| Economy of inclusion | An alternative framework based on solidarity and the common good, ensuring nobody is left behind. |
| Throwaway culture | A wasteful state-of-being that discards not only goods but also people who are no longer "useful" (Pope Francis). |

## Critical Citations

**Limits of relevance:**

- Qualitative phenomenological study, not quantitative; findings are interpretive rather than empirically measured.
- No specific algorithm or model is proposed; relevance is conceptual and behavioral.
- The paper focuses on critique of capitalism rather than design of financial systems; direct implementation guidance is limited.
- Data on Filipino spending (e.g., 87% statistic) comes from a single study (Lagman & Ocampo, 2017) on male impulse buying, not generalizable to all genders or updated.

## Limitations

- Relies heavily on unpublished interviews (e.g., Amy Cundangan, Kyle Ramos) and non-peer-reviewed online sources (Pinoy Stop, personal blogs) for behavioral claims.
- The 87% statistic is from a 2017 study on male impulse buying in a specific context; not representative of all Filipino young professionals.
- No empirical validation of the phenomenological linkage; causal claims are argued rather than tested.
- The paper's theological and moral framing may introduce bias in analyzing economic behavior. [unacknowledged]
- Limited discussion of income variability and how petsa de peligro differs across income levels or employment types.
