# Financial Literacy and Financial Health of Public Junior High School Teachers

## TL;DR (One Sentence)
Public junior high school teachers in Quezon City show high financial literacy but only “financially coping” health, with critical gaps in retirement planning, emergency funds, and wealth accumulation.

## Core Problem & Motivation
Teachers in the Philippines face low take‑home pay (≈₱23,465 after deductions) and rising living costs, yet limited research examines their financial well‑being in urban districts. This study assesses financial literacy and health to inform targeted training programs.

## Methods / Approach (Bulleted)
- **Design:** Quantitative‑descriptive, survey‑based.
- **Sample:** 241 public junior high school teachers from five schools in District VI, Quezon City (proportionate random sampling).
- **Instruments:** Modified Financial Health Network (2024) and Surendar & Subramanya Sarma (2018) questionnaires.
- **Analysis:** Means, frequencies, Mann‑Whitney U, Kruskal‑Wallis H tests (non‑parametric due to ordinal data).
- **Ethics:** Approved by PUP Research Ethics Committee (UREC‑2023‑0812), complied with Data Privacy Act.

## Key Findings / Results (Numbered or Bulleted)
1. **Financial literacy** overall mean = **4.02** (High; scale 1‑5). Highest in financial knowledge (4.24), lowest in retirement planning (3.75) and investment (3.86).
2. **Financial health** overall mean = **61.2** (Financially Coping; scale 0‑100). Sub‑scores: spending 63.17, saving **51.87** (Financially Vulnerable), borrowing 72.40, planning 60.22.
3. Only **40** on “I have money remaining at month end” (Financially Vulnerable) – most teachers live without surplus.
4. **Emergency fund** mean = 3.72 (out of 5) – many lack 6 months’ expenses.
5. **Debt** consumes large income share (78.44 on debt proportion indicator), leaving little for saving.
6. Significant differences in financial literacy by sex (females higher), age, number of children, income, education, position, experience, specialization.
7. Significant differences in financial health by sex, age, civil status, children, education, position, experience, specialization (e.g., borrowing higher for males).

## Definitions & Acronyms
| Term/Acronym | Definition |
|--------------|------------|
| **Financially Coping** | Meets daily expenses but can’t build long‑term security or wealth (score 50‑74 on Financial Health Network scale). |
| **Financially Vulnerable** | Struggles to meet basic expenses or has no surplus (score <50). |
| **Financially Healthy** | Consistent bill payment, savings, and planning (score ≥75). |
| **TLE** | Technology and Livelihood Education – most common specialization among respondents. |
| **PHP** | Philippine Peso (₱). |
| **Kruskal‑Wallis H / Mann‑Whitney U** | Non‑parametric tests for group comparisons (ordinal data, non‑normal distribution). |

## Critical Citations
- **Lusardi & Mitchell (2011)** – Establishes that financial literacy strongly predicts retirement planning; supports low retirement planning scores found here.
- **Villagonzalo & Mibato (2020)** – Found Philippine teachers have “good” management but poor financial attitudes; this study extends to urban Quezon City.
- **Bai (2023)** – Links financial planning and budgeting to financial health; echoes the “financially coping” profile observed.

## Limitations & Assumptions
- **Self‑reported data** – may overestimate literacy/health (social desirability bias).
- **Cross‑sectional** – cannot infer causality or track changes over time.
- **Single district** – results may not generalize to all Philippine public teachers.
- **Non‑normal distributions** – required non‑parametric tests, limiting some statistical power.

## Takeaway for Memory (≤ 3 bullet points)
- 🔑 **High literacy ≠ good health** – teachers know finance but live paycheck‑to‑paycheck (only 39/100 have month‑end surplus).
- ⚠️ **Retirement and emergency funds are weakest** – retirement planning scored 3.75/5; emergency fund 3.72/5 – *like knowing you need a lifeboat but never buying one.*
- 📌 **Debt dominates income** – 78% of monthly income goes to debt payments, leaving little for saving or investing.
