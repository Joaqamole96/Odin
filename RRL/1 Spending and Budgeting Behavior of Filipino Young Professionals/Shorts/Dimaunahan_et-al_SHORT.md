# Financial literacy and sustainable planning assessment among Filipino millennials

## TL;DR (One Sentence)
Financial literacy is very low among Filipino millennials and has no direct effect on financial planning, but income, occupation, and expenses do.

## Core Problem & Motivation
Only 25% of Filipino adults are financially literate, yet no recent studies examine how literacy affects financial planning in Filipino millennials. The study addresses this gap using goal setting theory to assess planning behavior.

## Methods / Approach (Bulleted)
- Survey of **400 Filipino millennials** (28–43 years old) in Makati City (non‑probability purposive sampling).
- Measured financial literacy via **“The Big Three”** (interest compounding, inflation, risk diversification) [Lusardi & Mitchell, 2011].
- Measured financial planning using **goal setting theory** moderators: commitment, specificity, acceptance, difficulty.
- Analyzed relationships with **partial least squares structural equation modeling (PLS‑SEM)**.
- Controlled for demographics: monthly income, occupation, monthly expenses.

## Key Findings / Results (Numbered)
1. **Financial literacy is very low**: Only 54.5% answered interest compounding correctly, 38.8% inflation, **33.8% risk diversification**.
2. **H1 not supported**: Financial literacy → financial planning β = **0.077** (p = 0.153) – *insignificant*.
3. **H2–H4 supported**: Monthly expenses (β = 0.205, p = 0.001), monthly income (β = 0.180, p = 0.003), occupation (β = 0.164, p = 0.004) all significantly affect financial planning.
4. All four goal‑setting moderators significantly affect financial planning (p < 0.001): goal acceptance (β = 0.924), commitment (β = 0.809), specificity (β = 0.597), difficulty (β = 0.212).
5. Majority of respondents (71.3%) employed full‑time; most common monthly income ₱10,001–₱30,000 (38.5%).
6. Convergent validity: Cronbach’s α > 0.70, AVE > 0.47 for all constructs.

## Important Figures & Tables (Condensed)
- **Figure 1** (conceptual framework): FL, demographics → financial planning (via goal commitment, specificity, acceptance, difficulty).
- **Figure 3** (final SEM): Monthly expenses has strongest direct effect (0.205); financial literacy removed due to insignificance.
- **Table 1** (demographics): 53% female, 67.5% bachelor’s degree, 71.3% full‑time employed.
- **Table 7** (hypothesis summary): H1 rejected; H2–H4 accepted; all higher‑order goal constructs accepted.

## Equations to Remember
None. The study uses path coefficients (β) from SEM, not explicit mathematical equations.

## Critical Citations
- [Lusardi & Mitchell, 2011] – Developed “The Big Three” financial literacy measure used worldwide.
- [Locke & Latham, 1990] – Goal Setting Theory foundation; framework for financial planning assessment.
- [Grohmann, 2018] – Found similarly low financial literacy (24% advanced) in emerging Asian middle class.

## Limitations & Assumptions
- Only **Makati City** – not representative of rural Philippines.
- Only **millennials** – results may differ for Gen X, boomers, or Gen Z.
- Single financial literacy measure (“The Big Three”) – may miss other dimensions.
- No mixed‑methods or qualitative follow‑up.

## Takeaway for Memory (≤ 3 bullet points)
- 🔑 **FL does not drive planning** – despite very low literacy (only 34% know risk diversification), Filipinos still plan using income, expenses, and job.
- 💡 **Expenses matter most** – monthly expenses (β=0.205) affect planning more than income (β=0.180) or occupation.
- 📌 **Goal acceptance is key** – accepting financial goals has the strongest effect (β=0.924) on actual planning behavior.
