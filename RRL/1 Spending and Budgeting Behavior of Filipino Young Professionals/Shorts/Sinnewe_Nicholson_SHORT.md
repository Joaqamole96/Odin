# Healthy financial habits in young adults: An exploratory study of the relationship between subjective financial literacy, engagement with finances, and financial decision-making

## TL;DR (One Sentence)
Social context (romantic relationships, past hardship) drives young adults’ financial habits more than financial literacy or confidence.

## Core Problem & Motivation
Young adults struggle with debt and low financial literacy, yet financial education programs show mixed results. We need to understand what actually shapes their financial habits during the transition into full‑time work.

## Methods / Approach (Bulleted)
- Semi‑structured interviews with 28 Australian university graduates (21–31 years, entered workforce within 5 years).
- Grounded theory analysis using Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB) and Family Financial Socialization (FFS) frameworks.
- Coded for habits, attitudes, influences, and financial outcomes.
- Average subjective financial literacy score: **6.4/10**; average financial satisfaction: **7.3/10**.

## Key Findings / Results (Numbered or Bulleted)
1. **Romantic partnerships shift time horizon**: Participants in committed relationships focus on long‑term goals (house, family) and use formal budgets or bucket systems.
2. **Motivation, not literacy, drives day‑to‑day engagement**: Subjective financial literacy only matters for event‑like decisions (e.g., investing).
3. **Past financial hardship** (childhood poverty, homelessness) creates strong future‑focused saving habits.
4. Parents are primary role models; peer discussions about money are rare.
5. **Only 7/28** perceive credit cards or buy‑now‑pay‑later as “bad debt”; most avoid debt entirely.
6. **21/28** are saving money; **10/28** use a formal budget; **20/28** have invested.

## Important Figures & Tables (Condensed)
- *Figure 1 (conceptual overview)*: Shows socialization (parents, partners, hardship) → money attitude → perceived norms → habit formation. Financial literacy only enables event‑like behaviors (investing, debt) via perceived control.
- *Table 2 (major themes)*: “Partner influences financial decision‑making” occurs in 13/28; “track expenses” in 26/28; “bucket system” in 16/28.

## Definitions & Acronyms
| Term/Acronym | Definition |
|--------------|------------|
| TPB | Theory of Planned Behavior – intention driven by attitude, norms, perceived control |
| FFS | Family Financial Socialization – how family shapes financial values and behaviors |
| Bucket system | Multiple sub‑accounts for specific savings goals (e.g., rent, emergency) |
| Subjective financial literacy | Self‑assessed financial knowledge (vs. objective test scores) |

## Critical Citations
- [Gudmunson & Danes, 2011] – Defines financial habits and family financial socialization theory.
- [Fernandes et al., 2014] – Meta‑analysis showing financial education explains only 0.1% of behavior variance.
- [Shim et al., 2009] – Links financial socialization to young adults’ well‑being.

## Limitations & Assumptions
- Sample is highly educated (72% bachelor’s degree vs. 44.5% national average) – not generalizable.
- Few participants reported debt, possibly due to self‑selection bias.
- Subjective literacy measure may not reflect objective knowledge.
- Qualitative design – causality cannot be proved.

## Takeaway for Memory (≤ 3 bullet points)
- 🔑 **Partner changes everything**: Being in a committed relationship creates future focus → formal budgets → investing (like a financial “accelerator pedal”).
- 💡 **Literacy isn’t the lever** – motivation is. Young adults already track spending, but need a reason (partner, hardship) to plan long term.
- 📌 **Parents install the default**: Parental role modeling shapes debt avoidance and saving habits. Peers rarely talk money – it’s “boring” or private.
