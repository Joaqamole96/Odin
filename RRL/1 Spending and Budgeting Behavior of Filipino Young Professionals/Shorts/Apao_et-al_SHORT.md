# Maritime Students' Financial Knowledge, Attitude, and Behavior

## TL;DR (One Sentence)
Maritime students have very high financial knowledge, attitude, and behavior, but neither knowledge nor attitude significantly predicts actual financial behavior.

## Core Problem & Motivation
Most financial literacy studies focus on knowledge alone, ignoring attitude and behavior – especially for Filipino maritime students who will soon earn large but irregular incomes. No research has examined how these three dimensions relate in this population.

## Methods / Approach (Bulleted)
- **Design**: Descriptive‑correlational (Latham & Radocy, 1995).
- **Sample**: 100 randomly selected maritime students from a private Philippine university (Ozamiz City).
- **Inclusion criteria**: Enrolled 1st semester 2023‑2024, weekly allowance ≥₱1,000, informed consent.
- **Instruments**: Three 4‑point Likert questionnaires (financial knowledge: 10 items; attitude: 10 items; behavior: 7 items) adapted from Roza (2024).
- **Analysis**: Mean, standard deviation, Pearson correlation.

## Key Findings / Results (Numbered or Bulleted)
1. Financial knowledge level: **Very high** (M = 3.74, SD = 0.14).
2. Financial attitude level: **Very high** (M = 3.71, SD = 0.10).
3. Financial behavior level: **Very high** (M = 3.85, SD = 0.17).
4. Knowledge vs. behavior: r = –0.46, p = 0.652 → **not significant**.
5. Attitude vs. behavior: r = 0.59, p = 0.558 → **not significant**.

## Important Figures & Tables (Condensed)
- **Table 1**: Knowledge mean (3.74) → “Very High” (all students consistently knowledgeable).
- **Table 2**: Attitude mean (3.71) → “Very High” (positive money mindset).
- **Table 3**: Behavior mean (3.85) → “Very High” (strong saving/budgeting habits).
- **Table 4**: Knowledge–behavior correlation (–0.46, p=0.652) → no relationship.
- **Table 5**: Attitude–behavior correlation (0.59, p=0.558) → no relationship.

| OECD | Organisation for Economic Co‑operation and Development (defines financial literacy framework). |

## Critical Citations
- [OECD, 2012] – Defines financial literacy as three dimensions (knowledge, attitude, behavior).
- [Lusardi & Mitchell, 2014] – Found that knowledge alone doesn’t guarantee good behavior (theory of planned behavior).
- [Shah & Patel, 2020] – Emphasized that attitude mediates knowledge‑behavior gap.

## Limitations & Assumptions
- Self‑reported data (behavior may be overestimated).
- Single institution (limited generalizability).
- Cross‑sectional design (cannot infer causality).
- No qualitative probing (why knowledge/attitude fail to translate).

## Takeaway for Memory (≤ 3 bullet points)
- 🧠 **High scores ≠ action** – Students know and feel positive, yet no correlation with behavior suggests a “knowing‑doing gap.”
- 📌 **Knowledge alone is not enough** – r = –0.46 (negative but not significant) implies other factors (peer influence, family background, financial stress) drive behavior.
- 💡 **Teach application, not just theory** – Maritime schools need budgeting simulations, not just lectures, to turn attitudes into habits.
