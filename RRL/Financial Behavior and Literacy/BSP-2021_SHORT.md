# 2021 Consumer Finance Survey (Philippines)

## TL;DR (One Sentence)
At the height of the COVID‑19 pandemic, Filipino households earned ₱190k/year on average, spent most on food (57%), owned mainly non‑financial assets (e.g., homes, appliances), and only 29% carried debt.

## Core Problem & Motivation
There were critical data gaps on household wealth and indebtedness in the Philippines. The survey aims to inform evidence‑based economic policies (monetary, financial) by measuring income, expenditure, assets, liabilities, and demographic characteristics of Filipino households.

## Methods / Approach (Bulleted)
- **Sample**: 18,000 households nationwide, 90.1% response rate, all regions including BARMM.
- **Unit of analysis**: Primary Economic Unit (PEU) – the financially interdependent household subset.
- **Data collection**: Face‑to‑face interviews (CAPI) from March to December 2022, covering calendar year 2021 for income/expenditure.
- **Key variables**: Income sources, expenditure by PCOICOP, non‑financial assets (real estate, vehicles, appliances), financial assets (deposits, e‑money, insurance), liabilities (loans, credit card debt, past‑due bills).
- **Analysis**: Weighted estimates, Gini coefficient, Multiple Correspondence Analysis to identify correlations.

## Key Findings / Results
1. **Income**: Average annual income = **₱189,842** (median ₱144,000); Gini coefficient = **0.422** (significant disparity). Top 10% earned 11× bottom 10%.
2. **Expenditure**: Average annual spending = ₱230,905; **food at home** accounted for 55.4% of total expenditure (Engel's Law holds).
3. **Assets**: 96.6% owned appliances (mobile phones: 92.8%), 69.9% owned homes, 35.3% owned vehicles (61.7% motorcycles). Financial asset ownership **doubled from 2018** to 50.4%.
4. **Deposit accounts**: 35.3% of households owned an account (up from 9.7% in 2018), driven by basic deposit accounts and digital finance.
5. **Electronic money**: 24.3% owned e‑money wallets/prepaid cards (almost zero in 2018), boosted by government aid distribution.
6. **Debt**: Only **29.3%** held any debt (down from 40.4% in 2018). Past‑due household bills (16.4%, mostly electricity) were more common than formal loans (15.2%).
7. **Insurance**: 11.2% owned insurance/pension (mostly SSS), with average monthly premium ~₱700.
8. **Net worth**: Average net worth = ₱435,346; principal residence contributed 50.3% of total assets.

## Equations to Remember
No statistical equations in the paper; the main inequality measure used is the Gini coefficient:
$$ Gini = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (2i - n - 1) y_i}{n \sum_{i=1}^{n} y_i} $$
A value of **0.422** indicates moderate income inequality.

## Limitations & Assumptions
- **Recall bias**: Respondents might misreport income/expenditure from the previous year.
- **Under‑reporting**: Intentional hiding of asset/liability values (especially informal loans).
- **CV warnings**: Estimates with coefficient of variation >20% (yellow highlights) should be interpreted with caution.
- **COVID‑19 context**: Data reflect pandemic conditions, not necessarily a normal year.

## Takeaway for Memory (≤ 3 bullet points)
- 🔑 **Average income ₱190k, spending ₱231k** – households spent more than they earned, drawing down savings or aid.
- 💡 **Only 1 in 3 have a bank account** – but financial asset ownership doubled during the pandemic, driven by e‑money (24.3%).
- 📌 **Debt is low (29%)** – pandemic made households debt‑averse; past‑due bills (16.4%) are bigger than formal loans (15.2%).
