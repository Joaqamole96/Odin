# Decoding the eco-financial mindset: financial literacy, attitudes, and efficacy measures and the spending behavior of Filipino millennials

## TL;DR (One Sentence)
Positive financial attitude strongly predicts responsible spending among Filipino millennials, while financial literacy and efficacy show no direct link to spending.

## Core Problem & Motivation
Filipino millennials face financial vulnerability from education costs, economic uncertainty, and complex financial products, yet existing literature examines financial literacy, attitude, and efficacy in isolation rather than together.

## Methods / Approach (Bulleted)
- Survey of 431 millennials in Laguna, Philippines via Google Forms.
- Measured financial literacy (7 items), financial attitude (8 items), financial efficacy (10 items), spending (9 categories).
- Five‑point Likert scales for self‑reported knowledge and agreement.
- Structural Equation Modeling (SEM) and Confirmatory Factor Analysis (CFA) using Jamovi.

## Key Findings / Results (Numbered or Bulleted)
1. Strong positive correlations among financial literacy, attitude, and efficacy (β = 0.52–0.77, p < .001).
2. Spending behavior is **negatively** associated with financial attitude (b = -0.02963, β = -0.1759, p = 0.034).
3. Spending had no significant relationship with financial literacy (p = 0.477) or financial efficacy (p = 0.292).
4. **93.7%** of respondents spend ≤10% of income on beer/wine (lowest variability).
5. 42% spend ≥41% of total income (highest variability).

## Important Figures & Tables (Condensed)
- Table 1: Demographics → 62.9% male, 50.8% earn PHP 20k–27k, 38.5% primary goal is wealth creation.
- Table 5: Parameter estimates → Financial attitude ⇒ spending β = -0.1759 (significant negative).
- Table 6: Correlation matrix → Financial attitude correlates with efficacy (r=0.77) and literacy (r=0.52); spending correlates negatively with attitude (r=-0.14).

## Equations to Remember
None. The model uses regression coefficients, not formal equations.

## Definitions & Acronyms
| Term/Acronym | Definition |
|--------------|------------|
| Financial literacy | Knowledge and understanding of financial concepts (e.g., interest rates, credit). |
| Financial attitude | An individual’s feelings, self‑control, and opinions about money management. |
| Financial efficacy | Confidence in one’s ability to handle financial stress and make sound decisions. |
| TPB | Theory of Planned Behavior (Ajzen, 1991): attitudes, norms, and perceived control drive behavior. |
| SEM | Structural Equation Modeling – multivariate analysis of latent variables. |
| CFA | Confirmatory Factor Analysis – tests how well measured variables represent constructs. |

## Critical Citations
- [Ajzen, 1991] – Theory of Planned Behavior, theoretical backbone of the study.
- [Yanto et al., 2021] – Social media positively influences millennials’ financial literacy and attitude.
- [Sotiropoulos & d’Astous, 2013] – High financial attitude prevents overspending (supports main finding).

## Limitations & Assumptions
- Self‑reported data prone to recall and social desirability bias.
- Most respondents work in finance‑related jobs (auditing, banking, teaching), potentially inflating financial knowledge.
- Cross‑sectional design – cannot track changes over time.
- Geographic scope limited to Laguna, Philippines; may not generalize nationwide.

## Takeaway for Memory (≤ 3 bullet points)
- 🔑 **Attitude matters most** – a positive money mindset reduces spending more than knowledge or confidence alone.
- 💡 **Beer/wine spending is remarkably consistent** – 94% of millennials keep it ≤10% of income.
- 📌 **No direct literacy–spending link** – knowing finance doesn’t automatically change spending habits (contradicts common assumption).
