# The Influence of Financial Literacy on the Budgeting Practices among College Students in a Private Catholic School: Input for Student Literacy Program

## TL;DR (One Sentence)
Financial literacy strongly predicts college students' budgeting practices (r = 0.85), explaining 72% of the variance.

## Core Problem & Motivation
Many college students lack effective budgeting skills despite holding positive financial attitudes. Schools need evidence‑based programs that translate financial knowledge into real‑world budgeting behavior, especially in private Catholic institutions with unique socioeconomic contexts.

## Methods / Approach (Bulleted)
- **Design**: Quantitative descriptive‑correlational using survey questionnaires.
- **Sample**: 225 randomly selected students from St. Peter's College of Toril, Inc. (Philippines).
- **Measures**: Financial literacy (5 dimensions – awareness, attitudes, risk management, culture, knowledge/skills) and budgeting practices (4 dimensions – goal setting, control, decision‑making, behavior).
- **Analysis**: Descriptive statistics (means, SD), Pearson correlation, linear regression.
- **Reliability**: Cronbach’s α = 0.996 for all scales, indicating excellent internal consistency.

## Key Findings / Results (Numbered or Bulleted)
1. Students show **high financial literacy** (overall mean = 3.95/5). Strongest dimension: Attitudes Towards Finance (4.03); weakest: Managing Financial Risk (3.82).
2. Budgeting practices are also **high** (overall mean = 3.91/5). Decision‑making scores highest (4.03); financial control lowest (3.77).
3. There is a **strong positive correlation** between financial literacy and budgeting practices (r = 0.85, p < 0.001).
4. Regression shows financial literacy alone explains **72.3%** of the variance in budgeting practices (R² = 0.723, β = 0.896, p < 0.001).
5. Students struggle with consistent application (e.g., tracking expenses) despite good knowledge – a gap between theory and practice.

## Important Figures & Tables (Condensed)
- **Table 1** (Financial Literacy): Attitudes 4.03 (highest), Risk Management 3.82 (lowest). → *Students need more training on long‑term risk (insurance, investments).*
- **Table 2** (Budgeting Practices): Decision‑Making 4.03 (highest), Financial Control 3.77 (lowest). → *Self‑discipline in daily spending is the weakest link.*
- **Table 3** (Correlation): r = 0.850 (p = 0.000). → *Financial literacy and budgeting are tightly linked.*
- **Table 4** (Regression): B = 0.850, R² = 0.723. → *Each 1‑point gain in financial literacy raises budgeting by 0.85 points.*

## Equations to Remember
$$ \text{Budgeting} = 0.366 + 0.850 \times (\text{Financial Literacy}) $$

*The constant (0.366) represents baseline budgeting when financial literacy is zero. The slope (0.850) shows how much budgeting improves per unit increase in literacy.*

## Critical Citations
- **Lusardi & Mitchell (2011)** – *Financial Literacy Theory*: Foundational framework defining financial literacy as knowledge + application for personal finance decisions.  
- **Ajzen (1991)** – *Theory of Planned Behavior*: Explains why knowledge alone doesn't guarantee action – social norms and perceived control matter.  
- **Sanjeev (2023)** – Emphasizes budgeting as a core financial literacy skill that requires planning and allocation of income.

## Limitations & Assumptions
- **Self‑reported data** may overestimate actual financial literacy and budgeting habits.
- **No measurement of external factors** (e.g., family income, part‑time jobs, prior financial education).
- **Single institution** (private Catholic school) limits generalizability to other settings.
- **Cross‑sectional design** prevents causal conclusions – the strong correlation does not prove causation.

## Takeaway for Memory (≤ 3 bullet points)
- 🔑 **0.85 correlation** – Financial literacy and budgeting are almost inseparable; boosting one almost certainly lifts the other.  
- 💡 **Weakest link = financial control** (mean 3.77) – students know *what* to do but fail to track daily spending (*like a diet plan you never follow*).  
- 📌 **72% explained variance** – Most of students' budgeting success comes from financial literacy; targeted education can close the remaining gap.
