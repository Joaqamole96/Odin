Introduction
As a mid-career freelance violinist and studio teacher with
bachelor’s and master’s degrees in music, my formal financial
education has been minimal. When I was in my twenties, my
financial skills were adequate for my minimal responsibilities
(i.e., I did not own a house and I did not have a family to
provide for). As the years went on and my financial life
included such tasks as buying a house, paying down student
loans, and raising children, I read books and articles, listened
to podcasts, and learned a great deal about personal financial
literacy. But in 2016, my learning accelerated significantly
when we bought my husband’s dental practice. Managing it
demanded a high level of financial and business expertise, and
the workload was too much for him to handle on his own. As
I strengthened my business knowledge to support the success
of his practice, I saw parallels between his office and my work
as a classical musician. Applying that knowledge to my private
studio and freelance career resulted in greater financial
success for me. I recognize that not every independent
musician and studio teacher will have a great interest in
personal finance or small business strategies. Therefore,
I am dedicated to sharing my knowledge in the hope that
fellow musicians can achieve greater financial stability and
build sustainable long-term careers in classical music. When
there are more professionals available to perform, teach, and
mentor, future generations will experience the life-changing
benefits of classical music.
Today’s studio teachers face significant challenges such as
recruitment, retention, and remaining relevant in a society
that seems more and more focused on technology and the
virtual world. The most significant obstacles, which seem to
be the ones that cause most musicians to leave the profession,
are the financial challenges that come with self-employment
in the arts field. Employers provide steady income, pay taxes
on the employee’s behalf, and offer insurance and retirement
savings options. Self-employed musicians and studio teachers
do not have the same compensation packages as those with an
employer. Instead, they must develop strategies for budgeting
with unsteady income, develop and implement a retirement
savings plan, accurately pay taxes, and purchase insurance
plans, such as health, disability, and instrument. With
strategies in place, it is possible for self-employed musicians
to survive and thrive financially. In this article, we will look
at the challenges and obstacles and then create a roadmap for
long-term financial success.

Managing Cash Flow
It is essential to track your monthly income and expenses
(Stanley & Danko, 2010). Budgeting can often feel
overwhelming, leading many to avoid thinking about it
altogether. If all of the bills for the month are covered, a budget
does not matter, right? Think about the amount of time and
energy you devoted to earn each of your dollars; it is important
to give those dollars a purpose so they work for you. When
spent intentionally, each of your dollars will contribute to your
long-term financial success. Your dollars are your orchestra
and you are the conductor; find joy in the power you have to
direct them!
Today, there are many free and low-cost budget tools in the
form of online applications. Some connect to your bank and
credit card statements so little data entry is required on your
part. If you are comfortable with Excel or Google Sheets,
a simple spreadsheet is free and serves the same purpose:
tracking all money that comes in and goes out. Spending a
few minutes a day looking at your budget is all that is needed
to put you in control of the flow of your dollars.
When determining your monthly budget, a good starting
place is the commonly recommended 50/30/20 model
(Preston & Hanson, 2006, see Figure 1): 50 percent of your
income for needs, 30 percent for wants, and 20 percent for

savings. Your needs will include monthly necessities such
as food, housing, and transportation, and also semi-annual
payments such as insurance premiums and estimated tax
payments. Wants includes everything you do not need but
enjoy, such as subscription services, clothes, and eating
at restaurants. Where to put your savings dollars will be
determined by the Financial Sequence of Success, which will
be outlined and explained shortly. (If you cannot save 20 % of
your income today, don’t panic! Start with whatever you can
save today, and increase little by little until you get to 20%.)
A successful business owner knows both their monthly
overhead and anticipated revenue for the month. Borrowing
from the business world, it can be helpful to work backward
when looking at monthly and annual cash flow. How much
money do you need to make each month/year to meet all
of your financial obligations, and how much do you have
coming in? Looking at your cash flow versus overhead
puts you in a position of control when determining how
to manage your time. For example, if you know you need
to earn $4,000 per month and you have $3,600 coming
in, you need to add a new student to your studio or call
local contractors (i.e., churches, wedding venues, or other
musicians) you have worked for to see if you can pick up a
gig. Conversely, if you know you are going to earn $4,000,
you can say no to additional work with confidence, ensuring
you do not burn out. Let’s say a prospective new student
calls, and they sound like your ideal student. If you know you
do not have to teach them but choose to add them to your
studio because you are excited to teach them, you will have a
different relationship with that student than if you add them
to your already full schedule because you think you need the
money.

Creating a “Salary”
Once you have a firm understanding of your monthly cash
flow, you can set up your bank accounts to mimic a salaried
job. To accomplish this, you will need two bank accounts,
either at the same bank or two different banks (Barrett 2016,
Sloan 2022, see Figure 2). Deposit all of your income into Bank
A. Then pay yourself a bi-weekly salary from Bank A into
Bank B. If we continue with our example of a $4,000 monthly

budget, you will deposit $2,000 every two weeks into Bank B.
As long as you track your monthly income and make sure you
are earning at least $4,000 every month, you will be able to
pay yourself a consistent salary, thus making your cash flow
and budgeting much easier! Continue to pay yourself the same
amount, regardless of your actual income—remember this is
your salary determined on an annual basis by your budget, not
an account you spend down to $0 each month.
Should you find that extra cash accumulates in Bank A over
the course of the year, you will have happy choices to make at
the end of the year. For example, if you have an extra $2,000
at the end of the year, you may plan for a paid vacation the
following year! That is two weeks of salary you can afford
to pay yourself even if you do not have income for those
two weeks. Another possibility is to give yourself a year-end
bonus. Since all your needs have been met throughout the
year, you can use this bonus intentionally—perhaps allocating
half toward your retirement savings and using the other half
to purchase that new piece of furniture you’ve been wanting.
Keeping to your “salary” will give you choices and control
over your dollars, ensuring they are working for you. But
how can your dollars work for you? Let’s dig deeper into your
savings.

Financial Sequence for Success
Financial success takes many years to achieve, especially
for those with medium or lower income. When mastering
our instruments, we learn that small amounts of effort over
long periods of time equal great achievement. By applying
that same principle to our finances—small amounts of effort
over long periods of time—long-term financial security and a
comfortable retirement become attainable (Collins 2023). The
following five steps will help secure your financial health today,
tomorrow, and through your retirement. Spend a small amount
of time each day and/or week monitoring your budget and
using the sequence to determine where to put your 20 percent
savings. There is nothing fancy or complicated about these
steps, it just involves patience, dedication, and consistency.
While these steps are tailored to musicians who are largely self-
employed, the basic ideas are applicable to musicians with any
means of income.

Step One: Emergency Fund Covering Six
to Twelve Months Living Expenses

One of the most well-known financial gurus, Dave Ramsey,
recommends an emergency fund of $1,000 (Ramsey 2009,
Ramsey 1992). I think this is far too low for musicians who
support themselves or families and do not have paid leave
from an employer! Consider who relies on your income and
what would happen if you could not earn that income for a
period of time. For example, if you fell, broke your arm, and
needed surgery you may not be able to teach for three weeks
and you may not be able to perform for four months or more.
Your emergency fund needs to be able to cover the loss of
income and additional expenses you may have so you do not
incur debt while you are recovering. Therefore, I recommend
six to twelve months in your emergency fund. Going back to
our $4,000 monthly budget, that means your emergency fund
should have $24,000–$48,000. Building this up will take time
so do not worry about having it all saved today. Instead, focus
on making daily choices that will lead you to your goal. If
you are following the 50/30/20 model on your $4,000 budget,
you are allocating $800 per month toward savings. It will take
you 2.5 years to save $24,000 in your emergency fund. If you
end up with a couple thousand dollars extra in your Bank A
account at the end of the year, you can choose to put it toward
your emergency fund and hit your goal sooner. Little by little,
month by month, you will get there and then you will have
financial security, knowing that a car repair, a home repair,
or a period of lost income will not cause you and your family
financial disaster.
The choice of where to put your emergency fund is an
important one. You want your money to grow, yet be
accessible at any time. Therefore, the choices are a bank
savings account or an online high yield savings account. A
high yield savings account is a great option as these accounts
are currently paying close to 5 percent interest, substantially
more than regular banks that are paying 0.01 percent. If you
put $24,000 into an account earning 5 percent and are able to
leave it for ten years, you will have close to $40,000, assuming

the interest rate stays the same. Compounding interest over a
long period of time is one of your greatest tools for financial
success! The other advantage to having your emergency fund
in an online account, as opposed to a local bank, is that it
usually takes one to three days for the funds to transfer to
your regular checking account. If you might be tempted to
pull from your emergency fund for a non-emergency, such as
new boots or an extra vacation, the delay will give you time to
pause and consider your decision.

Step Two: Savings to Cover Insurance Deductibles

The deductible is the amount you are responsible for
before your insurance pays on your behalf. Include all of the
insurance policies you carry, such as auto, home, health, and
instrument. Your insurance deductibles must be saved in
addition to your emergency reserves and here is why. Let’s say
you are on your way to teach and you are in a car accident.
Your wrist is broken, you need surgery, and you will not be
able to work for a month. Your car needs repairs, you will
have medical bills, and your instrument was damaged in the
accident. In this scenario, you will need your emergency fund
to cover your living expenses until you can get back to work
but you will also need cash to cover the deductibles for your
car, health, and instrument insurance. Having your insurance
deductibles set aside in addition to your emergency fund will
protect your long-term financial security.

Step Three: Pay Off High-Interest Debt

I consider any debt with an interest rate above 5 percent to be
high-interest debt. (If you have debt with an interest rate under
5%, it can be financially advantageous to keep that debt if your
investments are earning more than 5%). As you work to pay off
these debts, take note of past choices you made that caused you
to accrue the debt, and consider changes you can make so the
debt does not recur. There are different strategies for paying
down debt. One strategy is to pay off the smallest total first,
then roll that payment into the next highest amount of debt. As
you pay off debts, the payments will become larger, helping you
to pay off the debt faster. This can be a very effective strategy
from an emotional standpoint as you will experience “wins” of
paying off debts more quickly since you are starting with the
smallest balances. A more financially savvy way to approach
debt is by the interest amount—pay off your highest interest
debt first as that is the debt that is costing you the most money.
Consider both options and choose the one that will help you
achieve your goal of paying off your high-interest debt.

Step Four: Max Out Retirement Savings

One of the advantages of having a single employer and
earning on a W2 is that most employers include a retirement
plan with automatic contributions as part of their benefits
package. When you do not have an employer managing your
retirement savings for you, it is critical that you take on this
task! Having a well-funded retirement account does not force
you to retire at a certain age; it provides you with financial
stability should you choose or be required to stop working.

of health or other reasons, not because they choose to do
so). Setting up a low cost Individual Retirement Account
(IRA) at an investment firm such as Vanguard or Fidelity
is quick and simple: do it yourself online or call customer
service for assistance. It is important to remember that an
IRA is an Individual Retirement Account—regardless of your
relationship status, you should have your own retirement
account. The IRS does not view these as jointly held assets
and neither should you! Having your own retirement account
maximizes your tax savings and provides for you in the future.
The primary types of retirement accounts are Roth and
Traditional IRAs. Think of Roth and Traditional IRAs as two
different types of vessels where you will be able to choose how
to invest your money. The difference between these vessels
is when and how the money is taxed. With a Roth IRA, you
pay income taxes on the money today at your current tax rate,
invest it how you choose, and you do not pay any taxes on the
funds when you withdraw the money in retirement, including
the principal (the money you put into the account) and the
growth. If you are young and invest for a long period of time,
there can be a significant amount of growth that you will not
have to pay taxes on! This is an excellent retirement account
option, but there are income limits. As of tax year 2024, if
you make more than $161,000 (or $240,000 as a couple), you
cannot contribute to a Roth IRA. A Traditional IRA does
not have any income limits, and the tax structure is reversed.
When you contribute to a Traditional IRA you receive a
tax deduction today, but when you withdraw the money in
retirement you will be taxed on both the principal and growth.
The amount one can contribute to their IRA changes year
to year. Visit irs.gov to search for the current year’s limits,
divide that number by twelve, and add it as a line item to
your monthly budget. If you are not able to contribute the
maximum today, start with what you can contribute and
create a plan to increase to the maximum over the next few
years. Within your retirement account, you can choose how
to invest your money. Vanguard and Fidelity both offer low
cost, broad-based mutual and index funds. For example,
VTSAX is Vanguard’s Total Market Fund—it mirrors the US
stock market meaning the performance will reflect that of the
US stock market. The fee is 0.04 percent; in other words, it
is basically free. This is the type of fund you can confidently
buy and hold for many years; over the last thirty years, the
return has been over 10 percent which is solid (Bogle 2017).
For more information on simple portfolios you can manage
yourself, I recommend reading The Simple Path to Wealth
by JL Collins (Collins 2016). If you choose to use a financial
advisor, look for a fee-only financial advisor who will charge
you a set amount or hourly rate to design your portfolio. If
you opt for a broker to actively manage your accounts, you
will likely pay at least 2 percent (1% or more of the value of
your portfolio to the broker and at least 1% in management
fees on the funds they purchase), which can have a significant
impact on your long-term savings.
Depending on the type of health insurance plan you have,
another retirement vehicle may be available to you! The

health insurance plans with the lowest monthly costs, or
premiums, are usually high deductible plans. These plans
cover basic preventative care, but if you need to be treated
for an illness or injury, you will have to pay a large deductible
before your insurance kicks in. To help you pay your
deductible, you can save money in a health savings account,
or HSA. While not designed as retirement accounts, they can
function as such if you are able to pay for your medical care
without pulling money from your HSA. Some HSA providers,
such as Fidelity and HealthEquity, allow you to invest some
or all of your funds into the stock market, thus allowing your
money to grow over time. In 2024, the contribution limit was
$4,150 (or $8,300 for a family insurance plan); the funds must
be used to cover medical expenses, but there is no time limit
on when you need to use the funds. In other words, if you
have a medical bill tomorrow but are able to cash flow it, you
can reimburse yourself for that bill with money from your
HSA at any time in the future as long as you have the receipt
to file with your taxes.
An HSA can provide a triple tax advantage: the funds
you deposit are a tax deduction, if you invest the funds in
your account into the stock market the growth is tax free,
and when you withdraw money for eligible medical costs
they are not taxed. If you are over 65, you can withdraw
from your HSA for non-medical reasons without penalty
and the money will be taxed at your current income rate,
which will likely be low if you are retired. If used in this way,
your HSA will function the same as a Traditional IRA, but
instead of putting away just $7,000 per year you are able to
save over $11,000 per year (per 2024 contribution limits).
Compounded over your working years, this will have a huge
impact on your retirement savings. If you intend to let your
HSA money grow over time, make sure the HSA provider
you select allows you to invest in low cost, broad-based
index funds.

Step Five: Accumulation

If you have reached step five of this sequence,
congratulations! You are on very solid financial footing and
have achieved financial success! Now that your debts are
paid off, you are able to withstand financial emergencies, you
have adequate insurance and can cover your deductibles, and
your retirement savings are growing, you can make choices
regarding how you want to spend your extra earnings each
year. Some options include saving for your kids’ college
expenses, making home improvements, paying off your
mortgage early, or even taking a sabbatical. An unexpected
bill, such as a car repair or a broken water heater in your
home, will not send you into debt or cause you to struggle to
pay your bills that month. You have the resources to cover the
unexpected and you can see a wonderful retirement on the
horizon.

Conclusion
Intentional decisions, made every day throughout your
working years, will give you financial stability, even if you
