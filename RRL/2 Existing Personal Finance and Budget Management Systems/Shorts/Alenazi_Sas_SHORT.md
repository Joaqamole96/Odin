# Creating and Managing Transactions and Budgets: Analysis of Marketplace Descriptions and Functionality Review of Budgeting Apps

## TL;DR (One Sentence)
Functionality review of 45 top budgeting apps reveals 8 core features and 3 budget-management strategies, with most apps lacking theoretical grounding and clear cost descriptions.

## Core Problem & Motivation
HCI research has explored financial practices but largely ignored commercial budgeting apps, despite their rapid growth. This study addresses the gap by analyzing app descriptions and actual functionalities to inform better design.

## Methods / Approach (Bulleted)
- Searched Google Play and Apple Store (UK) for free budgeting apps using terms "budget", "budgeting", "finance".
- Initial 1335 apps reduced to 45 based on: >1000 reviews, rating ≥4.0, no bank account requirement.
- First author analyzed marketplace descriptions (metadata, content, modalities, cost, age, literacy).
- Both authors performed expert evaluation of actual app functionalities on Android and iOS.
- Iterative coding: deductive (prior HCI work, mental accounting theory) and inductive (observed features).

## Key Findings / Results (Numbered or Bulleted)
1. **App descriptions**: 98% in Finance category; only 53% available on both platforms; 87% have in-app purchases (often undisclosed); 44% claim suitability for children but privacy policies contradict.
2. **Eight core functionalities**: (1) transaction accounts (income, expense, saving), (2) transaction types (income, expense, transfer), (3) manual/scheduled entry, (4) security (PIN: 41 apps, biometric: 24), (5) transaction management (categorizing, tagging, search, export, status), (6) single vs multiple budgets, (7) budget visualizations (bar/doughnut/pie/line charts), (8) three budget management strategies.
3. **Three budget strategies**: Awareness of ongoing spending (23 apps), warnings before reaching limits (8 apps), signals for overspending (9 apps) – *like a speedometer showing green/orange/red zones*.
4. Most apps support **multiple budget categories** (26 apps) aligned with mental accounting theory, but 19 use only a single budget.
5. **Security gaps**: 4 apps have no PIN; 21 apps no biometric; only 35 support data backup.
6. **Export functionality** available in only 24 apps (14 as premium).
7. **Progress bars** for budget awareness: neutral colors (green/white) for within budget, orange for approaching limit, red for overspending.
8. **Notifications** for approaching/exceeding limits used by <25% of apps.

## Important Figures & Tables (Condensed)
- **Table 1 (functionalities summary)** → Most apps support expense/income transactions (45/44) but only 11 support savings accounts.

## Critical Citations
- [Thaler, 1999] – Foundational mental accounting theory; explains why people budget by categories.
- [Alenazi & Sas, 2023] – Prior review of budgeting apps (limited to tracking vs budgeting).
- [Kaye et al., 2014] – Showed people prefer analog tools (envelopes, jars) over digital for budgeting.

## Limitations & Assumptions
- Only free apps with >1000 reviews and rating ≥4.0 – excludes paid or less popular apps.
- UK marketplaces only – findings may not generalize globally.
- Expert evaluation (not real users) – actual usage patterns might differ.
- Did not assess app effectiveness or long-term user adoption.

## Takeaway for Memory (≤ 3 bullet points)
- 🔑 **8 features, 3 strategies** – Remember: accounts, transactions, entry, security, management, budgets, visuals, strategies + ongoing/warning/overspend.
- 💡 **87% hide true cost** – "Free" apps often have undisclosed subscriptions (avg £17.31). Check before downloading.
- 📌 **Envelopes work** – Multiple budgets (26 apps) align with mental accounting; single-budget apps (19) ignore proven theory.
