# CHAPTER II
# REVIEW OF RELATED LITERATURE AND STUDIES

This chapter presents a comprehensive exploration of existing research and scholarly works relevant to the development of Odin: A Personal Budget Management System Using LSTM, Classification Algorithm, and Recommendation Algorithm. The review is organized thematically, covering ten major conceptual territories—from target user behavior and system design precedents to the individual algorithmic modules and evaluation methodology. For each subtopic, literature from international and local sources is examined and critically analyzed, followed by a synthesis that identifies methodological patterns and research gaps that Odin's development directly addresses.

## A. Target Users and the Financial Problem

### A.1 Spending and Budgeting Behavior of Filipino Young Professionals

Filipino young professionals represent the primary user segment that Odin is designed to serve. Understanding their income structures, budgeting behaviors, financial failure points, and culturally specific financial obligations is essential for grounding the system's design decisions in empirical local context rather than generic Western assumptions.

A growing body of literature documents the diversity of income arrangements among Filipino workers in the millennial and Gen Z range. Torres, Bartolome, Jimena, and Paner (n.d.) provide direct empirical comparison between traditional employees and gig workers across five Philippine provinces, finding that traditional employees carry a significantly higher median debt-to-income ratio (40) compared to gig workers (31.41), while financial literacy scores are statistically equivalent between groups (median 9 for both, p = 0.267). This challenges the assumption that employment type predicts financial sophistication and confirms that Odin must serve both stable-income and variable-income users with equivalently intelligent support. Casalhay, Guevarra, and Bragas (n.d.) deepen this picture by documenting that Metro Manila freelancers face persistent income instability from client cancellations, delayed payments, and seasonal fluctuations, and while many adopt percentage-based allocation strategies (e.g., 30% to savings, 70% to expenses), these methods are frequently overwhelmed by unpredictable cash flows. Velez (2025) adds the systemic dimension, noting that GCash has reduced cash dependency by 41% in urban Philippines, normalizing digital financial management among the population Odin targets, while simultaneously revealing that only 34% of low-income users understand digital security features. Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas (2021) provides the most authoritative national baseline: Filipino households averaged ₱189,842 in annual income in 2021, allocated 55.4% of total expenditure to food at home, and showed 92.8% mobile phone ownership against only 35.3% bank account ownership—figures that directly validate Odin's mobile-first, manual-input architecture and its FIES-grounded expense categorization.

The literature on budgeting practices among Filipino young adults consistently reveals a knowledge-behavior gap. Dimaunahan et al. (n.d.) find that monthly income (β = 0.205) and monthly expenses (β = 0.180) predict financial planning behavior among Filipino millennials more strongly than financial literacy, which shows no significant direct effect (p = 0.153). Goal acceptance (β = 0.924) and goal commitment (β = 0.809) emerge as the strongest psychological drivers. Dela Torre et al. (n.d.) corroborate this from a college context, reporting a strong positive correlation (r = 0.7247) between budgeting skills and financial stability among Filipino students, with budget planning, goal-setting, and expense tracking as the three core components—yet also noting that a substantial minority fail to adhere consistently. Cacnio and Romarate (n.d.) reveal a paradox at the national level: Filipino young adults (ages 18–39) have the highest financial literacy index score (0.392) among age groups, yet higher literacy correlates with a lower likelihood of spending within income (OR 0.435, p < 0.01). These findings converge on a design implication: Odin should prioritize behavioral scaffolding over financial education delivery. Apao et al. (n.d.) reinforce this by showing that among Filipino maritime students, financial knowledge and financial behavior are not significantly correlated (r = −0.46, p = 0.652), confirming that self-assessed literacy is a poor proxy for spending discipline. Espiritu (n.d.) finds that among Filipino business administration students, financial literacy knowledge, attitudes, and practices are strongly correlated with each other (R = 0.697) and vary significantly by age, sex, and income—supporting Odin's decision to profile users along these demographic dimensions. Am-una (n.d.) adds that public school teachers in Baguio City exhibit "conscious constraint"—disciplined management of scarce resources—rather than genuine financial security, and that marital status and employment rank significantly differentiate financial behavior (p = 0.034), further validating demographic profiling. Cabiles (n.d.) and Bongalonta, Bongalonta, and Gigantoca (n.d.) document similar patterns among Filipino professional employees: strong budgeting and savings intentions coexist with inconsistent practice, with 60% of state university faculty saving only 0–15% of income and the majority following expense-first budgeting rather than saving first.

Documented expenditure patterns reveal that Filipino spending is deeply shaped by cultural obligations beyond what standard Western budgeting frameworks account for. Ama (n.d.) analyzes the 2023 FIES data for 163,268 households, confirming that food is the dominant expenditure (29.5% of food spending on bread, 14.8% on meat, 14.2% on fish) and that income elasticity for food is 0.58—a necessity-category behavior that sets realistic anchors for Odin's budget recommendation logic. Albert, Briones, and Rivera (n.d.) report that middle-class households (39.8% of Filipinos by 2021) allocate 8.8% of expenditure to transportation, 3.2% to health, and 1.3% to education, and that OFW remittances account for 8.5% of total middle-class income—irregular inflows that Odin's forecasting and anomaly detection modules must accommodate. Bayangos and Lubangco (n.d.) show that remittance-receiving households exhibit fundamentally different budget behavior: 50% allocate nothing to savings, and over 90% of cash remittances are spent directly on consumption. Rosario (n.d.) documents that indigenous Filipino households in Mountain Province treat kinship obligations (og-ogfo, ritual contributions) as non-negotiable, prioritizing them over personal savings—a "protected category" pattern directly applicable to how Odin should handle culturally obligated expenses. Amrith (n.d.) situates the "breadwinner mentality"—the belief that all earnings must support the family—as a deeply ingrained pattern that Odin's budget recommendation module must accommodate rather than override. Casilan and Baclagan (n.d.) clarify that lower-middle-income Filipino parents view children's financial contributions as voluntary (kusang loob) rather than obligatory debt, but children's top aspiration remains helping parents financially—an expectation that shapes how young professionals allocate discretionary income. Casiño, Serrano, and Granada (n.d.) add that utang na loob operates as both social glue and potential financial burden, with young Filipino adults simultaneously affirming its value and labeling its misuse "toxic."

The prevalence of periodic, culturally driven spending spikes is a related and underexplored dimension. Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas (2026 Q1 CES) reports that Filipino consumer spending intentions vary significantly by quarter, with Q2 outlook declining most sharply for food (−7.4 ppt) and transportation (−4.0 ppt), reflecting macroeconomic pressures that Odin's forecasting module must integrate as contextual signals. Amado (n.d.) documents that among Filipino teachers paid semi-monthly, the perceived financial well-being score is 4.18 (Agree), yet stress between pay periods persists (mean 3.63), and teachers prioritize essential expenses over non-essentials (mean 4.46) as their primary coping strategy—evidence that the bi-monthly pay cycle structures a specific temporal budgeting pattern that Odin must accommodate. Aguilar et al. (n.d.) show that Filipino micro-enterprises with strong budgeting practices have significantly higher profitability (rho = 0.536) and liquidity (rho = 0.526), while cash flow monitoring correlates even more strongly with sales growth (rho = 0.648), underscoring the practical financial value of the systematic tracking Odin facilitates. Delos Santos et al. (n.d.) reveal that Filipino micro-entrepreneurs in public markets struggle specifically with categorizing fixed versus variable costs (AWM 3.20, the lowest in expense tracking), and Alvarina et al. (n.d.) show that below-minimum-wage workers in Pagadian City allocate first to childcare (10/10 priority) and food (9/10) before any discretionary spending.

Comparative studies illuminate the behavioral differences that emerge across income stability types. Torres et al. (n.d.) demonstrate that gig workers, despite lower income certainty, maintain lower debt loads—potentially reflecting more cautious financial behavior under uncertainty. Ong, Keh, Lui, Santos, and Suarez (n.d.) find that myopic decision-making significantly reduces business performance among low-income micro-entrepreneurs in Manila (p < 0.001), and that access to finance moderates this negative effect (p = 0.005)—a finding that supports Odin's budget recommendation module as a form of "access to future funds" that can mitigate present bias. Avelino et al. (n.d.) report that income level has a highly significant effect on financial management and lifestyle modifications among solo parents (χ²(5) = 47.4, p < .001), with lower-income individuals engaging in more aggressive budget cutting. The convergent picture across these studies supports Odin's decision to treat income stability as a primary profiling dimension.

On the question of intelligent tools versus literacy interventions, several papers argue persuasively for the primacy of tool-mediated behavioral support. Ying and Blaise (n.d.) synthesize big data analytics research showing that consumer spending is driven by cognitive biases and emotional factors rather than rational economic calculation—a finding that justifies Odin's behavioral profiling over knowledge-based design. Breza and Kaur (n.d.) demonstrate from development economics that soft commitment devices (labeled lockboxes, appointment reminders) are as effective as hard financial penalties, and that planning fallacy causes households to underestimate irregular future expenses by 43%—making Odin's forecasting module a cognitive aid as much as a prediction engine. Aoun et al. (n.d.) find that financial attitude (β = 0.414) is a stronger predictor of financial behavior than financial literacy (β = 0.337) or FinTech adoption (β = 0.144) among millennials in a crisis economy, and that attitude fully mediates the literacy-behavior link—implying that Odin must foster positive attitudes through intuitive UX rather than relying on users' pre-existing knowledge.

Local contextual studies provide essential grounding for all of the above. The D, R, G (n.d.) study on working students under inflation shows that Filipino young people prioritize food, utility bills, and school materials, cut non-essential expenses, and pack lunch to save—coping mechanisms that mirror young professionals' strategies. Nduka and Benedicto (n.d.) find that 53.57% of Filipino nurses support four or more dependents, with single breadwinners reporting "barely anything left" after family support—a structural income-expense deficit that Odin's anomaly detection should recognize as a profile pattern rather than overspending. Cervantes et al. (n.d.) document a strong correlation (r = 0.612, p < 0.05) between online buying decisions driven by social media exposure and poor budget management among Filipino students, with age and civil status significantly differentiating saving behavior (p < 0.05). Anaya et al. (n.d.) report that implicit peer pressure explains only 7.45% of variance in impulsive buying among Filipino senior high school students, with deal-seeking (M = 3.43) as the dominant trigger—a finding that confirms peer influence is not the primary driver of spending anomalies and that promotional spending patterns deserve more weight in anomaly detection logic. Ataza et al. (n.d.) find that psychological aspects (β = 0.430, p = 0.006) and interest rate variations (β = 0.878, p < 0.001) significantly predict saving patterns among working Filipino millennials, while social aspects have a negative effect (β = −0.182, p = 0.016). Co and Centeno (n.d.) use Theory of Planned Behavior data to show that subjective norms (household member with a bank account, 10.16 pp effect) and perceived behavioral control are stronger predictors of financial intentions than attitudes—supporting Odin's design to leverage social proximity and personal efficacy rather than persuasive messaging. Calanog et al. (n.d.) add that Filipino young adults using Shopee Pay Later primarily for essentials develop planning strategies (pre-payment tracking, needs-over-wants prioritization) that Odin's recommendation module can formalize and scale. Avelino et al. (n.d.), Peso por Peso, quantify income-level differences in financial management among solo parents, providing a demographic reference baseline for income-stratified profile classification. Bongado, Magallanes, and Semaña (n.d.) find that digital wallet usage among Filipino HEI students explains 45.4% of variance in financial behavior (R² = 0.454, p < .001), with perceived trust showing the widest variability (SD = 1.24)—underscoring that trust-building is a prerequisite, not an afterthought, for Odin's adoption.

**Synthesis.** The studies reviewed under A.1 collectively construct a detailed and locally grounded empirical portrait of the Filipino young professional as a financial actor. Methodologically, the reviewed works range from nationally representative survey data (BSP CFS 2021, FIES 2023, BSP CES 2026) to targeted quantitative studies (Dimaunahan et al., Torres et al., Cacnio & Romarate), qualitative investigations (Casalhay et al., Casilan & Baclagan, Casiño et al.), and comparative analyses (Ong et al., Avelino et al.). Together, they show that Filipino young professionals have statistically equivalent financial literacy across employment types, face structurally driven spending pressures (family obligations, semi-monthly pay cycles, cultural events, debt), and consistently fail to translate financial knowledge into consistent budgeting behavior—a gap that behavioral tools rather than educational content are better positioned to close. The evidence that income type, marital status, goal commitment, and social norms are stronger predictors of financial behavior than literacy per se provides a direct empirical foundation for Odin's behavior-driven user profiling module.

Several critical gaps emerge from this body of literature. Most studies are cross-sectional and unable to track how budgeting behavior evolves over time or in response to tool-mediated interventions. Quantitative studies on Filipino spending patterns are skewed toward specific professions (teachers, nurses, micro-entrepreneurs) and institutional contexts, with limited coverage of private-sector young professionals in Metro Manila—Odin's primary user segment. Culturally specific financial behaviors (paluwagan, utang na loob, seasonal spending spikes) are well-documented qualitatively but rarely measured in ways that can inform algorithmic design directly. These gaps make it imperative for Odin to approach its user profiling and budget recommendation modules with cultural sensitivity, behavior-driven classification logic, and the flexibility to accommodate income volatility and obligation-driven spending that Western personal finance frameworks do not anticipate.

### A.2 Existing Personal Finance and Budget Management Systems

A substantial body of literature examines the landscape of existing personal finance management systems (PFMS) and personal budget management systems (PBMS), identifying both their strengths and the persistent gaps that motivate the development of systems like Odin.

Alenazi and Sas (n.d.) provide the most focused evaluation, finding that only 26 of 45 top-rated budgeting apps (58%) implement envelope-based multiple budgets, and that one-third provide only expense tracking with no budgeting functionality. Most apps fail to distinguish between income accounts (money-in) and expense accounts (money-out), creating cognitive confusion, and 33 of 35 apps that support transfers allow them even when source accounts have insufficient funds—a design flaw that undermines budget integrity. Patel and Singh (n.d.) describe an AI-based personal finance framework that validates the hybrid rule-based plus machine learning approach for transaction classification and demonstrates that dynamic budgeting derived from historical spending improves adherence, but is limited to Indian UPI users without any mobile-specific performance evaluation. Badiger et al. (n.d.) present Spend AI, a Next.js-based system achieving 91.3% F1-score for transaction categorization using XGBoost across 18 categories, with a Gemini-powered RAG insight pipeline that 84% of users find useful—but again in an Indian digital payment context (UPI-centric) without coverage of Filipino spending patterns. Bashshar et al. (n.d.) demonstrate an NLP-based system achieving 86.2% categorization accuracy using Random Forest with TF-IDF, with ARIMA forecasting producing a 30-day expense MAE of 253.47 on six months of historical data—useful as a baseline comparator for Odin's LSTM module. Bhavana, Pavan, and Darshan (n.d.) validate a multi-module architecture closely paralleling Odin's, with Isolation Forest and Autoencoders achieving 95% anomaly detection accuracy and a 22% improvement in savings in a 100-user pilot. Chahar et al. (n.d.) report an AI-powered personal finance assistant with a user satisfaction score of 4.4/5 and ease of use of 4.5/5, with forecasting accuracy rated 4.2/5—providing a prototype precedent for Odin's multi-module design. Yadav et al. (n.d.) extend this with an integrated platform that achieved 94% receipt-scanning accuracy and user ratings above 4.6/5 across 50 users, demonstrating that AI-driven insights improve perceived financial control. Imawan et al. (n.d.) evaluate an Android-based PFM app with income/expense tracking, budget projection, and goal setting that scored 4.6/5 overall from 50 young adults, with 78% rating their intention to continue at 5/5—directly validating Odin's feature set without requiring gamification.

D'Souza, Bhegade, Bhalekar, and Bhavsar (n.d.) provide the most comprehensive comparative survey of ML techniques for PFMS, establishing that LSTM captures long-range temporal dependencies through gated memory and is the preferred model for expenditure sequences, while Isolation Forest is the preferred unsupervised anomaly detection method for personal finance because it operates without labeled data and assumes anomalies are rare and structurally distinct. Al-E'mari, Sanjalawe, and Al-E'mari (n.d.) synthesize the broader AI-in-finance landscape, reporting that machine learning-based anomaly detection systems outperform traditional rule-based detection by 35%, and that RPA implementations achieve 50–60% efficiency gains in routine financial tasks. Whitaker (n.d.) and Ying and Blaise (n.d.) both confirm from a big data analytics perspective that predictive analytics on historical transaction data can forecast spending patterns and that clustering algorithms can segment users by spending habits without labels—foundational validations of Odin's forecasting and profiling modules.

Several papers address the specific limitations of existing systems that motivate Odin's design. Elliyana, Maricar, and Toalib (n.d.) find in their systematic review that while structured budgeting reduces impulsive spending in 47 studies, financial literacy without self-regulation mechanisms fails to produce sustained behavior change. Cho (n.d.) documents that young adults find manual budgeting "tedious, time-consuming, and stress-inducing," leading to abandonment—a friction point that automated categorization and intelligent nudges must address. D'Acunto and Rossi (n.d.) synthesize evidence that income aggregators displaying a complete balance sheet reduce nondurable spending and overdraft fees, goal-setting via robo-advisors increases monthly savings by €60, and overspending alerts reduce daily spending by 5% with cumulative non-reverting effects—directly justifying Odin's anomaly detection and budget recommendation modules as behavioral intervention tools. Balcazar-Paiva, Haro-Sarango, and Villanueva-Calderón (n.d.) identify that the AI-in-finance literature lacks standardized evaluation metrics and longitudinal evidence—a gap that Odin's ISO 25010 + SUS evaluation plan is positioned to address. Efendi and Widagdo (n.d.) find that informal financial actors (Indonesian housewives) naturally develop three-tier expense prioritization (primary > secondary > tertiary) through mental accounting—validating the envelope budgeting approach that few existing apps fully implement. Chepkutwo (n.d.) argues that frugal fintech design focusing on 4–5 core functions achieves higher adoption among financially anxious users, directly countering the assumption that feature richness drives engagement.

**Synthesis.** The literature on existing PFMS and PBMS reveals a consistent pattern across methodologically diverse studies: most systems prioritize expense tracking over budgeting, offer generic categories that do not reflect local spending norms, lack predictive or anomaly detection capabilities, and underperform specifically for user populations with variable income or culturally specific financial obligations. The evaluations reviewed—from expert audits of app stores (Alenazi & Sas) to prototype user studies (Imawan et al., Chahar et al., Yadav et al.) to large-scale systematic reviews (D'Souza et al., D'Acunto & Rossi)—converge on the finding that intelligent, adaptive features (forecasting, anomaly alerts, personalized recommendations) demonstrably improve financial behavior, yet these remain the exception rather than the norm in available systems. When AI-powered systems do exist, they are almost universally designed for non-Filipino contexts, relying on bank API integration unavailable to the majority of Philippine users and employing expense categories that do not reflect FIES or BSP Consumer Finance Survey structures.

The central research gap is the absence of a locally grounded, mobile-first PFMS that combines intelligent multi-module design (forecasting, anomaly detection, categorization, recommendation, and profiling) within a manual-input architecture suited to users who are unbanked or bank-averse, have variable income, and carry culturally specific financial obligations. The behavioral research consistently demonstrates that the impact of financial tools depends on how closely they match users' actual spending contexts—a condition that existing international systems fail to satisfy for Filipino young professionals. Odin addresses this gap directly by grounding its design in FIES/BSP categories, targeting the income and cultural characteristics documented in the Philippine literature, and combining multiple intelligent modules within a mobile-first interface.

## B. Mobile-First Design and Deployment Constraints

### B.1 Mobile-First Design in Personal Finance Systems

The decision to design Odin as a mobile-first application is not merely a technical preference but an empirically grounded necessity given the Philippine digital infrastructure context and the preferences of the target user demographic.

The case for mobile-first dominance in Southeast Asia is well-supported. Velez (2025) reports that GCash has reduced cash dependency by 41% in urban Metro Manila, demonstrating that digital mobile payment platforms are the financial infrastructure of choice for Filipino young adults. The BSP CFS (2021) confirms that 92.8% of Filipino households own mobile phones, while only 35.3% hold bank deposit accounts—a penetration asymmetry that makes mobile the only viable delivery channel for an application targeting financially underserved or bank-averse users. Arinze (n.d.) argues from East African mobile money literature that mobile-first databases with offline sync, real-time updates, and strong encryption are critical for reliable app performance in low-bandwidth environments—conditions that parallel Metro Manila's variable internet connectivity. Chepkutwo (n.d.) reinforces this with evidence that frugal, offline-resilient fintech design achieves higher adoption among financially anxious users, and that tiered onboarding (starting with only a phone number) reduces exclusion.

The literature on mobile UX design for personal finance provides concrete principles for Odin's interface decisions. Jamal and Hashmat (n.d.) document that mobile UI/UX is shifting toward AI-driven personalization, gesture interactions, and inclusive design, while struggling with small screen real estate, cross-platform consistency, and rapidly evolving user expectations—challenges that Odin must address in its front-end design. Faisal et al. (n.d.) synthesize 112 studies showing that user convenience (24/7 availability, easy navigation, fast transactions) is the single most important driver of digital banking adoption (cited in 47 studies), followed by transparent security measures—directly informing Odin's UX priority ordering. Ashrafuzzaman et al. (n.d.) show that AI-driven personalized services produce a 19-percentage-point higher 90-day retention rate compared to non-personalized controls, and that behavior-based segmentation using K-means clustering replaces demographic profiling in effective digital finance apps. The AI-Enabled NLP Framework paper by Jayaprakashnarayan et al. (n.d.) demonstrates that on-device 8-bit model quantization reduces model size by 4× while preserving 98.7% of accuracy, enabling sophisticated NLP on smartphones with 43–127ms latency and 0.9–2.0%/hour battery drain—a concrete proof of concept for deploying intelligent modules in a mobile context.

Technical constraints on mobile platforms directly influence algorithm selection for Odin's embedded ML modules. Hall and Rasheed (n.d.) find that LightGBM matches or exceeds LSTM accuracy on large datasets while training 100–1,000× faster, making it the computationally efficient default when data volume is sufficient—a consideration for Odin's long-term architecture. Song et al. (n.d.) warn that complex deep learning models often underperform simpler linear models on sparse datasets due to overfitting, and that extending the lookback window beyond the optimal range degrades accuracy—a constraint directly applicable to Odin's manual-input transaction data. Ma et al. (n.d.) propose that hybrid LSTM-CNN models can capture both short-term local patterns and long-term behavioral dependencies more efficiently than pure LSTM, offering a direction for Odin's future mobile optimization.

The BSP CFS and CES data confirm that the digital divide is real but narrowing, and that mobile is the appropriate convergence point for financial inclusion among Filipino young professionals. Bongado, Magallanes, and Semaña (n.d.) show that digital wallet usage explains 45.4% of variance in financial behavior among Filipino HEI students, with perceived ease of use, usefulness, and trust as the key adoption determinants. Imawan et al. (n.d.) validate that an Android-based PFM app with manual transaction entry can achieve Excellent usability ratings (4.6/5) from a young adult population without requiring banking integration or complex onboarding.

**Synthesis.** The reviewed literature converges on a clear and consistent finding: mobile-first design is not a design preference for Odin but a contextual imperative. The Philippine financial infrastructure—characterized by high mobile penetration, limited bank account ownership, variable internet connectivity, and a young, digitally active target demographic—creates a deployment context in which a desktop-first or bank-integrated architecture would systematically exclude a large portion of intended users. The methodological range across these studies, from large-scale national surveys (BSP CFS, CES) to systematic reviews of digital adoption (Faisal et al., Velez) to technical benchmarks on on-device ML (Jayaprakashnarayan et al., Hall & Rasheed, Ma et al.) and usability evaluations (Imawan et al., Jamal & Hashmat), provides multi-layered justification for Odin's design philosophy.

The primary research gap visible in this literature is the absence of studies that specifically evaluate mobile-first personal finance systems with embedded ML modules in the Philippine context. International findings on mobile finance design and on-device ML optimization provide useful methodological transfer, but the specific interaction between Filipino digital infrastructure constraints (device capabilities, connectivity patterns, screen literacy) and the performance of PFMS modules like spending forecasting or anomaly detection has not been empirically studied. Odin's development and evaluation will contribute directly to addressing this gap, providing evidence on how a locally designed, mobile-first, intelligent PFMS performs under Philippine deployment conditions.

## C. Budget Recommendation

### C.1 Budgeting Strategies and Budget Recommendation

Budget recommendation is a core module of Odin, designed to translate users' financial profiles and spending histories into actionable, adaptive allocation guidance. The literature on budgeting strategies and how they are implemented in intelligent systems provides essential grounding for this module's design.

Multiple systematic reviews establish budgeting as the foundational behavior that underpins savings, debt reduction, and financial stability. Elliyana, Maricar, and Toalib (n.d.) synthesize 47 studies showing that structured budgeting reduces impulsive spending and builds saving discipline, and that digital financial tools increase saving frequency when users have baseline digital literacy—but that financial literacy alone, without self-regulation mechanisms, fails to produce sustained behavior change. Bai (n.d.) provides a specific psychological mechanism, finding that mental budgeting—the cognitive practice of categorizing and tracking expenses—has a significant direct effect on financial well-being (β = 0.102, p < 0.001) independent of financial literacy and self-control, validating Odin's per-category budget structure as a behavioral intervention. Dela Torre et al. (n.d.) and Dimaunahan et al. (n.d.) confirm in Filipino contexts that goal-setting is among the strongest predictors of budget adherence (β = 0.924 for goal acceptance in Dimaunahan et al.), directly informing Odin's recommendation module to frame budget allocations as goal-commitments rather than static limits.

A significant subset of the literature evaluates specific budgeting methods and how they translate into digital implementations. Alenazi and Sas (n.d.) document that envelope-based multiple budgets are implemented in only 58% of top-rated apps—a market gap that Odin fills. Efendi and Widagdo (n.d.) find that even untrained financial actors naturally develop three-tier prioritization hierarchies (primary > secondary > tertiary) analogous to envelope budgeting, suggesting that this structure reflects genuine cognitive financial organization and will feel intuitive to users. de Zarzà et al. (n.d.) present a linear optimization framework that maximizes savings under income and expense constraints, where user-defined preference weights allow category-level personalization—a direct algorithmic precedent for Odin's constrained budget recommendation. D'Acunto and Rossi (n.d.) synthesize evidence from robo-advisor literature that goal-setting increases monthly savings by €60 for target users and that overspending alerts reduce daily spending by 5% cumulatively—demonstrating the effectiveness of the behavior-feedback approach Odin adopts.

Several Philippine-context studies document the budgeting strategies that Filipino users actually employ and the gaps these leave. Aguilar et al. (n.d.) find that budgeting adherence correlates strongly with profitability (rho = 0.536) and liquidity (rho = 0.526) among Filipino micro-enterprises, while the weakest practice is adjusting cash flow plans when needed (mean 2.58/4)—pointing to the need for adaptive, automated recalculation. Casalhay, Guevarra, and Bragas (n.d.) document that Metro Manila freelancers commonly try percentage-based allocation (30/70 splits) but find these approaches reactive and insufficient under income volatility. Amado (n.d.) shows that semi-monthly pay cycles improve short-term planning but do not eliminate inter-period stress—a temporal feature that Odin's recommendation module must accommodate through flexible time-horizon support. Nduka and Benedicto (n.d.) and Alvarina et al. (n.d.) both confirm that Filipino professionals and low-income workers employ strict priority hierarchies under budget pressure (essentials first, discretionary last)—behavioral baselines that Odin's recommendation engine can use as default allocation templates. Delos Santos et al. (n.d.) show that micro-entrepreneurs prefer informal saving mechanisms (paluwagan, AWM 3.22) over formal deposits—a finding that argues for Odin's recommendation module to provide paluwagan-aware budgeting support.

From a theoretical and algorithmic standpoint, the budget recommendation literature offers multiple candidate approaches. Aboud (n.d.) demonstrates that goal programming balances conflicting financial objectives (revenue, expenses, savings, asset constraints) with near-zero deviation using preemptive priority ranking and weighted deviation minimization—directly translating to Odin's problem of allocating income across categories while respecting non-negotiable floors. Gulbakyt et al. (n.d.) compare Sequential Quadratic Programming (SQP) and Genetic Algorithm (GA) for multi-criteria budget optimization, finding that SQP converges in under 100 iterations (suitable for real-time mobile recalculation) while GA provides global robustness at higher computational cost. Lu, Zhou, and Zhang (n.d.) contribute a constrained, data-driven budgeting framework that uses saturation functions to model diminishing returns in spending categories and Monte Carlo simulation to report constraint-satisfaction probabilities—a design direction for Odin to provide probabilistic budget confidence intervals. Hashemi, Darejeh, and Cruz (n.d.) find that axiom-based explanations for budget allocations improve user understanding in 72.4% of participants and increase perceived fairness in 30%, but decrease trust in 23%—a critical finding that Odin's recommendation UI must account for by measuring, not assuming, explanation impact on trust. Yang (n.d.) demonstrates that fuzzy K-means clustering combined with neural collaborative filtering achieves Precision@5 = 0.85 for financial product recommendations and adapts in real time to changing user behavior, providing an algorithmic template for adaptive, behavior-driven budget recommendation.

Broader systematic reviews of AI in personal finance provide contextual support. D'Souza et al. (n.d.) compare rule-based budgeting (transparent, low-cost, but fails on seasonal patterns) against LSTM-based methods (adaptive, accurate, but lower interpretability), recommending hybrid approaches with post-hoc attribution. Dewi (n.d.) synthesizes Gen Z literature showing that fintech features (auto-debit, real-time transparency, gamification) improve saving discipline through reduced friction and positive reinforcement—but Odin's non-gamification design philosophy aligns more closely with the value-driven retention findings (78% reduced manual effort in Badiger et al., 41% recommendation adoption in Bhavana et al.) than with extrinsic reward systems.

**Synthesis.** The literature on budgeting strategies and recommendation algorithms reveals a well-developed theoretical foundation (goal programming, linear optimization, multi-criteria methods) alongside consistent empirical evidence that behavior-oriented, automated budgeting tools outperform static or knowledge-based approaches. Studies span methodological approaches ranging from mathematical optimization models (Aboud, Gulbakyt et al., Lu et al.) to systematic reviews of behavioral evidence (Elliyana et al., D'Acunto & Rossi) to Philippine-specific field studies (Aguilar et al., Casalhay et al., Amado). The convergent message is that effective budget recommendation must be adaptive (responsive to spending history and profile), constraint-aware (protecting essential categories and respecting income limits), culturally grounded (accommodating Filipino spending obligations), and accompanied by transparent explanations that do not inadvertently erode trust.

A notable gap in this literature is the absence of budget recommendation systems that have been empirically validated on Filipino spending data, with FIES/BSP-aligned categories, and in a mobile-first deployment context. International optimization models are mathematically sound but rely on user-defined preference weights that may not be realistic for users without financial training, and they have not been tested under the specific income volatility patterns (semi-monthly pay, gig income, irregular remittances) documented in the Philippine context. ### C.2 Budget Recommendation Algorithm

The selection and design of the specific algorithm underlying Odin's budget recommendation module requires a review of candidate approaches, their empirical performance, and their suitability for the mobile-first, low-data-entry context that characterizes Odin.

(n.d.) present a linear programming framework where LLM-generated allocation percentages serve as feasible initial solutions, constrained by income, non-negativity, and total-expense bounds, and weighted by user preference per category—producing personalized savings-maximizing allocations. Although Odin does not integrate an LLM, the optimization structure itself is directly implementable with rule-based or user-defined initial bounds. Gulbakyt et al. (n.d.) provide the comparative benchmark: SQP converges to optimal budget allocation in under 100 iterations with a Gini coefficient of 0.223 (equitable cross-category distribution), while GA matches this result (0.0007% difference) after 500 generations—the trade-off clearly favoring SQP for real-time mobile recalculation. Aboud (n.d.) demonstrates the power of goal programming for multi-objective allocation: preemptive ranking satisfies higher-priority targets (savings, essential spending) before lower ones (discretionary), and the weighted deviation method allows penalizing overspending more severely than undersaving—a nuanced control mechanism that Odin can implement through profile-specific priority configurations.

Algorithmic approaches from related domains provide additional candidate methods. Bhavana et al. (n.d.) demonstrate that a contextual bandit and reinforcement learning recommendation engine achieves a 41% adoption rate for personalized budget suggestions in a 100-user pilot, suggesting that sequential decision-making algorithms can produce meaningfully personalized recommendations without requiring large labeled datasets. D'Souza et al. (n.d.) review rule-based, ML, and hybrid approaches for PFMS budget recommendation, concluding that rule-based methods are transparent and low-cost but fail to adapt to seasonal or multi-feature spending patterns (producing high false-positive rates during holidays or one-time purchases), while LSTM-based methods are adaptive but lower in inherent interpretability. Lu, Zhou, and Zhang (n.d.) contribute the insight that budget allocations should incorporate diminishing-return saturation functions to model the declining marginal utility of spending beyond a category threshold—analogous to how Odin's recommendation engine should flag that adding budget to an already-saturated category yields little behavioral benefit. Hashemi et al. (n.d.) warn that explanation mechanisms for budget recommendations can backfire: while 72.4% of users found axiom-based explanations helpful, 23% experienced reduced trust—a finding that argues for optional explanation transparency rather than mandatory disclosure in Odin's UI.

**Synthesis.** The budget recommendation algorithm literature presents a clear landscape of candidate approaches: linear programming (de Zarzà et al.), goal programming (Aboud), multi-criteria optimization with SQP/GA (Gulbakyt et al.), fuzzy clustering with collaborative filtering (Yang), and reinforcement learning (Bhavana et al.). The consistent finding across studies is that any effective recommendation algorithm must respect hard income constraints, protect essential spending categories through minimum floors, adapt to changing user behavior over time, and communicate its recommendations in a way that builds rather than erodes trust.

The primary gap in this literature is the absence of algorithms explicitly designed and validated for the Filipino spending context—particularly for users with semi-monthly pay cycles, irregular cultural obligations, and variable income from side hustles. Existing optimization models assume static preference weights and do not accommodate the concept of "protected" obligation-driven categories (paluwagan, remittances, SSS contributions) that Filipino users treat as non-negotiable. Odin's recommendation algorithm must be designed to treat these categories as constrained floors rather than variables, a design requirement that existing literature motivates but does not yet implement.

## D. Predictive Modeling and Spending Forecasting

### D.1 Predictive Modeling in Personal Finance Systems

The spending forecasting module is one of Odin's most technically sophisticated components, requiring a review of predictive modeling approaches across personal finance and adjacent domains to justify the choice of LSTM and to contextualize its expected performance.

The theoretical case for applying deep learning to personal spending forecasting is well-established in adjacent domains. Aguiar-Pérez and Pérez-Juárez (n.d.) systematically compare LSTM against GRU, CNN, and ARIMA on energy demand time series data, finding that a hybrid CNN-LSTM achieves the lowest MAPE (0.514% versus SVM's 14.32%), and that LSTM's forget gate mechanism enables it to learn which past patterns to retain—directly applicable to spending cycles with monthly periodicity. Chowdhury, Paul, and Rozony (n.d.) review 72 studies on retail e-commerce demand forecasting and find that LSTM reduces MAPE by up to 20% over ARIMA for volatile, high-frequency data with seasonal patterns, and that hybrid ARIMA-LSTM models offer improved robustness during demand shifts—analogous to income shocks in personal spending. Lee et al. (n.d.) provide the most methodologically relevant comparison for Odin: sequential deep learning models (LSTM, CNN-LSTM hybrids) reduce prediction error by 10–20% over regression for longitudinal expenditure data, with prior spending consistently identified as the strongest predictor. Ma et al. (n.d.) extend this finding to household-level time series, confirming that LSTM solves gradient vanishing problems through memory cells and forget gates, and that bottom-up forecasting (per-category prediction then aggregation) achieves higher accuracy than direct total spending prediction—a design pattern Odin adopts.

Comparative reviews provide important cautions against overconfidence in LSTM. Song et al. (n.d.) find that complex deep learning models often underperform simple linear models (DLinear) on certain datasets due to overfitting, particularly when lookback windows are extended beyond optimal ranges—a risk Odin must test empirically given its sparse manual transaction input. Hall and Rasheed (n.d.) synthesize 79 studies showing that LSTM ranks first in 58.97% of deep learning comparisons but that LightGBM matches or exceeds its accuracy while training 100–1,000× faster on large datasets, and that on small datasets (0–2,173 samples) RNNs outperform gradient-boosted trees by 50 percentage points—directly relevant to Odin's cold-start scenario. Kontopoulou et al. (n.d.) confirm that ML models outperform ARIMA on non-linear time series by up to 40× lower RMSE, but that ARIMA remains superior on small linear datasets, supporting a hybrid or fallback strategy for new Odin users with minimal transaction history. Tjøstheim (n.d.) synthesizes M4 and M5 competition results, finding that hybrid methods combining statistical and ML components outperform pure approaches, and that LightGBM dominated the M5 competition on intermittent, zero-inflated data—a pattern analogous to Odin's per-category transaction data where many categories may have zero spending in a given period.

Domain-specific applications in personal finance confirm the feasibility of the forecasting approach. Badiger et al. (n.d.) show that Prophet time-series forecasting on 4,200 transactions produces per-category expenditure predictions with 90% credible intervals using only three months of history—a baseline comparator for Odin's LSTM module. Bashshar et al. (n.d.) establish ARIMA as a statistical baseline with MAE 253.47 and RMSE 318.91 on six months of spending data, providing a lower-bound benchmark. Thakur and Jadhav (n.d.) demonstrate that a voting ensemble regressor achieves 78.11% R-squared for expense prediction on household transaction data, outperforming individual models including XGBoost (77.89%), and that preprocessing steps including log transformation and TF-IDF vectorization improve ML-based expense tracking performance. Whitaker (n.d.) and Ying and Blaise (n.d.) both confirm that predictive analytics on historical transaction data is a viable approach for personal spending forecasting, and that clustering algorithms can enhance forecasting by segmenting users into behavioral profiles that inform model initialization. Duvalla (n.d.) adds that RNN implementations achieve higher predictive accuracy for future financial behaviors than conventional ML, and that behavior analytics using transaction frequency and spending sequences are the preferred segmentation basis in digital finance.

The evaluation methodology for forecasting models is well-established in the literature. Kara and Şengüler (n.d.) find that MAPE (used in 38 of 69 studies) and RMSE (36 studies) are the most common metrics for forecasting accuracy in budget contexts, with focused multi-model comparisons (3–6 methods) producing the most credible evidence. Pagliaro (n.d.) warns that standard k-fold cross-validation fails for time-series data due to information leakage, and that combinatorial purged cross-validation or walk-forward validation is required for valid performance estimation—a methodology Odin's evaluation plan must adopt. Breza and Kaur (n.d.) add the behavioral dimension: planning fallacy causes households to underestimate irregular future expenses by 43%, making the forecasting module a correction for this systematic cognitive bias rather than merely a technical prediction tool.

**Synthesis.** The literature on predictive modeling in personal finance systems provides a convergent empirical case for LSTM as the appropriate algorithm for Odin's spending forecasting module, with the necessary caveats that simpler baselines (ARIMA for cold-start, LightGBM for larger datasets) should be benchmarked and that walk-forward validation is essential for honest performance assessment. The methodological diversity across the reviewed studies—ranging from large-scale systematic reviews (Aguiar-Pérez & Pérez-Juárez, Chowdhury et al., Hall & Rasheed) to multi-domain comparisons (Song et al., Tjøstheim, Kontopoulou et al.) to domain-specific PFMS applications (Badiger et al., Bashshar et al., Thakur & Jadhav)—provides multi-layered evidence for algorithm selection rather than relying on a single study.

A critical gap in this literature is the absence of personal spending forecasting studies using Filipino young professional data, with manual-input transaction sparsity, and evaluated on FIES-aligned categories. The nearest analogues (energy demand, retail e-commerce, healthcare expenditure) share time-series properties but differ in behavioral drivers, data frequency, and cultural context. Odin's forecasting module will need to demonstrate empirically—on its own transaction dataset—that LSTM's advantage over simpler baselines persists under the sparse, manually entered, category-level spending data of its target user population. This constitutes an original empirical contribution that no existing study in the reviewed literature has provided.

### D.2 LSTM as the Spending Forecasting Algorithm

The selection of Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks as Odin's primary spending forecasting algorithm requires specific justification beyond the general case for deep learning, given the computational constraints of mobile deployment and the sparse nature of manual transaction data.

The core architectural justification for LSTM is well-established. Aguiar-Pérez and Pérez-Juárez (n.d.) show that LSTM's memory gates enable the model to selectively retain long-term spending patterns while discarding short-term noise, a mechanism that is particularly suited to monthly spending cycles with irregular spikes. Ma et al. (n.d.) confirm that LSTM solves gradient vanishing and explosion problems through its cell state architecture, making it suitable for irregular spending sequences with variable inter-event timing. The review by D'Souza et al. (n.d.) establishes that LSTM networks capture long-range temporal dependencies in expenditure sequences through gated memory mechanisms, with the limitation that they require substantial historical data and offer low inherent interpretability—a trade-off Odin accepts in favor of accuracy. George, Alam, and Hasan (n.d.) add cross-domain validation: in the fraud detection literature, LSTM and RNN architectures capture sequential dependencies in transaction streams, outperforming traditional models across 21 reviewed studies with over 7,300 citations.

Comparative benchmarks clarify where LSTM excels and where it may be outperformed. Hall and Rasheed (n.d.) find that LSTM ranks first in 58.97% of deep learning comparisons across 79 studies, but that LightGBM is 100–1,000× faster to train—a critical trade-off for Odin's mobile deployment. Song et al. (n.d.) warn that Transformer-based models degrade accuracy with longer lookback windows due to overfitting, while LSTM remains competitive and better suited for shorter sequences where recursive state matters—an advantage for Odin's monthly per-category forecasting horizon. Tjøstheim (n.d.) cites evidence that LSTM and MLP neural networks produce the best volatility forecasts among all models tested on S&P 500 data (over 10 years), and that Transformers and TCNs train faster due to parallelization but LSTM's sequential state processing is advantageous for shorter, variable sequences. Kontopoulou et al. (n.d.) confirm that deep learning models achieve up to 40× lower RMSE than ARIMA on non-linear series, and that hybrid ARIMA-LSTM models improve accuracy by 20–35% over standalone approaches—motivating Odin to test a hybrid fallback for cold-start users. Kara and Şengüler (n.d.) show that LSTM's average forecasting horizon of 3.3 years makes it suitable for short-term spending prediction, while linear models (AR, MA) are better for long-horizon projections. Pagliaro (n.d.) adds the important caution that Extra Trees achieve 86.1% directional accuracy against LSTM's 53.2% for medium-horizon (10-day) predictions in financial markets, suggesting that Odin's forecasting module must benchmark LSTM against tree-based ensembles before assuming superiority. Lee et al. (n.d.) provide the most domain-relevant evidence: LSTM and CNN-LSTM hybrids reduce RMSE and MAE by 10–20% over regression methods on longitudinal expenditure data specifically, with prior spending as the strongest predictor—confirming that LSTM's sequential processing provides genuine value for spending forecasting beyond what tree-based models can capture.

Compagnino et al. (n.d.) document from fraud detection literature that even well-tuned Random Forest achieves only 0.34 recall on real banking data with extreme class imbalance, justifying LSTM's complementary role: unlike Random Forest, LSTM can model the temporal structure of spending sequences rather than treating each transaction as an independent observation. Chowdhury et al. (n.d.) provide the quantitative justification for LSTM over ARIMA on volatile, non-linear data (up to 20% MAPE reduction), while Boniol et al. (n.d.) establish that LSTM-based anomaly detection uses prediction error as a detection signal—suggesting that Odin's single LSTM model can serve dual purposes in forecasting and anomaly detection pipeline.

**Synthesis.** The literature on LSTM as a spending forecasting algorithm presents a nuanced but ultimately supportive case. The key finding across studies is that LSTM's advantage over alternatives (ARIMA, tree-based ensembles, simple DNNs) is most pronounced on sequential, longitudinal, non-linear data with long-range temporal dependencies—precisely the characteristics of per-category personal spending time series. The caveats are equally clear: LSTM requires historical data to outperform simpler models, is computationally more expensive than LightGBM or ARIMA, and should be validated on out-of-sample data using walk-forward rather than k-fold cross-validation. The reviewed studies collectively justify LSTM as Odin's primary forecasting algorithm while specifying the benchmarking protocol and fallback conditions (ARIMA for cold-start, LightGBM consideration as data grows) necessary for honest scientific evaluation.

A gap that this literature does not address is the specific performance of LSTM on manually entered, sparsely sampled, per-category personal spending data from Filipino young professionals. All reviewed LSTM studies use automated, high-frequency transaction streams (bank APIs, IoT sensors, smart meters, credit card logs)—a data regime fundamentally different from Odin's manual, lower-frequency input. This gap represents the core empirical contribution of Odin's forecasting module evaluation, and the reviewed literature provides the methodological framework (MAPE, RMSE, MAE, walk-forward validation) within which that contribution will be assessed.

## E. Anomaly Detection

### E.1 Anomaly Detection in Personal Finance Systems

Odin's anomaly detection module is designed to flag unusual spending patterns—budget category overages, sudden behavioral shifts, and atypical transaction clusters—without relying on labeled fraud data. This requirement places Odin's anomaly detection firmly in the domain of unsupervised behavioral anomaly detection rather than supervised fraud classification.

The systematic reviews of anomaly detection methods provide the theoretical foundation. Boniol et al. (n.d.) organize a decade of time-series anomaly detection research into distance-based, density-based, and prediction-based methods, documenting a surge in deep learning approaches after 2016, with prediction-based methods (LSTM, autoencoders) representing nearly 50% of new methods between 2020 and 2023. Wang et al. (n.d.) survey 46 deep learning models for multivariate time series anomaly detection, proposing a taxonomy of point-wise, pattern-wise, and inter-metric anomalies—a framework applicable to distinguishing single-transaction overages (point-wise) from behavioral drift (pattern-wise) in Odin's alert logic. Zhou and He (n.d.) define rare category examples as compact, statistically significant anomalies distinct from noise—justifying a two-stage approach in Odin: filter noise first, then detect behavioral outliers. D'Souza et al. (n.d.) confirm that unsupervised anomaly detection methods dominate PFMS because labeled personal spending data defining "inefficient" behavior is practically unavailable for individual users.

The specific algorithms considered for Odin are well-supported by the reviewed literature. Multiple papers independently converge on Isolation Forest as the preferred unsupervised method for personal finance anomaly detection. Compagnino et al. (n.d.) establish that Isolation Forest isolates anomalies through random partitioning—anomalies require fewer partitions than normal points—enabling unsupervised detection without labeled training data. Bhavana et al. (n.d.) validate this empirically, reporting that Isolation Forest combined with Autoencoders detected anomalous transactions with 95% accuracy on real-world financial data. D'Souza et al. (n.d.) conclude that Isolation Forest is the preferred unsupervised method for PFMS specifically because it operates without labeled data, scales efficiently at O(n log n), and assumes anomalies are rare and structurally distinct—assumptions that hold for personal spending where budget overages are uncommon relative to normal transactions. George et al. (n.d.) add that hybrid models combining unsupervised anomaly detection with supervised classification reduce false positive rates while improving recall compared to single-paradigm approaches. Martínez, Sánchez, and Alcaraz (n.d.) demonstrate that Isolation Forest pre-filtering reduces data volume by 76% while capturing 94% of anomalies, enabling effective semi-supervised learning with only 1.2% labeled data—a finding directly applicable to Odin's scenario where initial user data is unlabeled.

Fraud detection literature, while not directly targeting personal spending anomalies, provides transferable methodological insights. Kalideen (n.d.) establishes that fraudulent transactions constitute less than 1% of all payment card transactions, creating severe class imbalance that requires specialized handling—a condition analogous to Odin's budget overages (rare relative to normal transactions). The survey emphasizes that ensemble methods outperform single classifiers on imbalanced datasets, and that federated learning enables personalization without centralized data. Majumder (n.d.) confirms that unsupervised anomaly detection (Isolation Forest, Autoencoders) identifies anomalous patterns without labeled training data, making them applicable to cold-start profiles. Odufisan, Abhulimen, and Ogunti (n.d.) show that real-time analysis of transaction sequences using RNNs can flag unusual spending before harm occurs, and that reducing false positives through continuous retraining is essential for user trust. Martínez et al. (n.d.) provide the key quantitative benchmark: LSTM networks achieve 89–92% AUC-ROC for sequential transaction anomaly detection but require 3–5× longer training than XGBoost, motivating a hybrid approach where Isolation Forest handles unsupervised pre-screening and LSTM handles sequential pattern detection.

The behavioral dimension of anomaly detection is addressed by Cervantes et al. (n.d.), who link social media exposure and instant gratification to unplanned purchases with a strong correlation (r = 0.612, p < 0.05) to poor budget management—suggesting that Odin's anomaly detection should be sensitive to promotional event periods and discount-driven spending spikes. D'Acunto and Rossi (n.d.) demonstrate that overspending alerts comparing current category spending to recent personal history reduce daily spending by 5%—validating the practical behavioral impact of the anomaly detection signals Odin will generate.

**Synthesis.** The reviewed literature provides strong and methodologically diverse justification for Odin's anomaly detection module. Systematic reviews (Boniol et al., Wang et al., D'Souza et al.) establish the theoretical taxonomy of anomaly types and algorithmic categories. Comparative benchmarks (Martínez et al., Bhavana et al.) validate Isolation Forest as the preferred unsupervised method for personal finance contexts with cold-start constraints. Fraud detection surveys (Kalideen, Majumder, Odufisan et al.) contribute transferable methodological insights on class imbalance, hybrid architectures, and evaluation metrics. And behavioral studies (Cervantes et al., D'Acunto & Rossi) confirm the practical relevance and behavioral impact of the anomaly detection signals Odin will produce.

A gap in the literature is the absence of anomaly detection systems specifically designed for behavioral budget adherence (as opposed to criminal fraud) in Filipino personal finance contexts. Existing methods are validated on banking transaction streams (automated, high-frequency, European or US datasets), not on manually entered, sparsely sampled, category-level spending records from Filipino young professionals. The specific challenge of distinguishing culturally obligated spending spikes (e.g., paluwagan contributions, Christmas expenditures) from genuine budget overages has not been addressed algorithmically—a design problem unique to locally grounded systems like Odin.

### E.2 Anomaly Detection Algorithm

The selection of the specific algorithm(s) for Odin's anomaly detection module requires examining the empirical performance and deployment characteristics of candidate methods in the context of Odin's constraints: no labeled training data, mobile-first deployment, manual transaction input, and cold-start users.

The case for Isolation Forest as Odin's primary anomaly detection algorithm rests on a convergent body of evidence. Compagnino et al. (n.d.) explain the mechanism: Isolation Forest randomly partitions the feature space, and anomalies (transactions that differ structurally from the normal spending distribution) are isolated in fewer partitions than normal points—a property that enables detection without any labeled data. Boniol et al. (n.d.) situate Isolation Forest within the broader taxonomy as a distance/partitioning-based method, distinct from the prediction-based (LSTM) and reconstruction-based (VAE, AE) methods that dominate recent deep learning anomaly detection. D'Souza et al. (n.d.) confirm that Isolation Forest operates at O(n log n) complexity, making it computationally feasible for mobile deployment, and that it assumes anomalies are rare and structurally distinct—assumptions verified by the personal spending context where budget overages are infrequent. Martínez et al. (n.d.) provide the quantitative validation: Isolation Forest pre-filtering captures 94% of anomalies while reducing data volume by 76%, enabling a semi-supervised two-phase architecture that is directly applicable to Odin's cold-start scenario.

Deep learning alternatives are reviewed to establish where they offer advantages and where they impose costs that Odin's constraints cannot absorb. Wang et al. (n.d.) survey reconstruction-based methods (VAE, AE, GAN) that model temporal dependencies without labeled data and offer entity-level anomaly explanation—attractive for Odin's transparency requirements—but at higher computational cost than Isolation Forest. Boniol et al. (n.d.) show that prediction-based methods (LSTM-NDT) using non-parametric dynamic thresholding on prediction residuals provide an integrated forecasting-and-detection pipeline, meaning Odin's LSTM forecasting module can be extended to serve anomaly detection through prediction error monitoring. George et al. (n.d.) validate the hybrid architecture empirically: models combining unsupervised pre-screening (Isolation Forest) with supervised or sequential pattern detection (LSTM) achieve both reduced false positives and improved recall—the optimal trade-off for a user-facing application where false alerts erode trust and missed overages erode value. Jayaprakashnarayan et al. (n.d.) demonstrate a concrete production implementation: an ensemble of rule-based screening, Isolation Forest, and LSTM autoencoder achieves 91.7% sensitivity and 3.8% false positive rate on personal transaction streams, with on-device deployment feasible at 43–127ms latency. Kalideen (n.d.) highlights the importance of explainable AI for anomaly alerts, noting that black-box models are problematic in high-stakes environments and that federated learning can preserve privacy while enabling model improvement. Majumder (n.d.) and Odufisan et al. (n.d.) both confirm that real-time, unsupervised detection is essential for personal finance contexts where labeled fraud data does not exist and where users expect immediate, actionable alerts rather than batch-processed reports.

**Synthesis.** The anomaly detection algorithm literature converges on a tiered recommendation for Odin: Isolation Forest as the primary unsupervised detection engine for its computational efficiency, cold-start capability, and strong empirical performance in financial contexts; LSTM-based prediction error monitoring as a complementary sequential detection method that reuses the forecasting module; and a hybrid ensemble (Isolation Forest + LSTM autoencoder, or rule-based screening + Isolation Forest) as the deployment-ready architecture, consistent with the highest-performing systems reviewed. The Precision-Recall trade-off in favor of recall (catching overspending) over precision (avoiding false alarms) is a domain-specific design decision that the literature supports explicitly, given that the cost of missing a genuine overage (financial harm) exceeds the cost of a false alert (minor user inconvenience).

A gap in this literature is the absence of anomaly detection algorithms calibrated specifically to distinguish culturally obligated spending spikes (seasonal events, paluwagan cycles, family contributions) from genuine budget deviations in Filipino personal finance. Existing methods treat all distributional outliers as candidate anomalies, which would generate false alerts whenever a Filipino user makes a large but culturally expected payment. Odin's anomaly detection module must be designed to suppress these alerts through cultural calendar awareness or user-confirmed exemption categories—a design requirement that the reviewed literature motivates but does not yet operationalize.

**Synthesis.** The literature on user behavioral profiling in Filipino personal finance contexts presents a rich, multi-layered empirical portrait of the behavioral diversity within Odin's target population. Studies range from nationally representative surveys (BSP CFS, BSP CES, Cacnio & Romarate) to targeted quantitative analyses (Dimaunahan et al., Torres et al., Ataza et al.) to qualitative investigations of cultural behavioral dimensions (Rosario, Casilan & Baclagan, Casiño et al.) and applied ML-based profiling systems (Duvalla, Hu et al., Yang). The convergent finding across this methodological diversity is that effective behavioral profiling for Filipino users must combine demographic variables (income type, marital status, employment category, age) with behavioral observations (spending patterns, category allocations, transaction timing) and cultural constructs (family obligation propensity, paluwagan participation, short-term orientation) to capture the distinct financial behavior types that Odin's recommendation and anomaly detection modules need to serve.

A critical gap is the absence of empirically validated behavioral profiles specific to Filipino young professionals in Metro Manila, constructed from actual transaction data rather than surveys. The existing profiles are either demographic (FIES income quintiles, BSP consumer segments) or industry-driven (banking customer segments)—neither is designed for the purpose of personalizing budget recommendations and anomaly detection for an individual user of a PFMS. Odin's profiling module represents a methodological contribution: the construction and validation of transaction-behavior-driven profiles grounded in Filipino financial data, aligned with FIES/BSP categories, and designed for adaptive real-time updating as user behavior evolves.

### F.2 Profile Classification Algorithm

The classification algorithm that assigns users to behavioral profiles at onboarding and updates those assignments as transaction data accumulates must balance accuracy, cold-start performance, computational efficiency, and interpretability—requirements shaped by Odin's mobile-first, manual-input deployment context.

The literature presents several candidate approaches. Hu, Qiu, Hu, Cheng, and Qiu (n.d.) demonstrate a K-means-based user profile system for a finance platform, showing that clustering enables precise segmentation based on income, expenditure, and transaction history—but note that K-means is unsupervised and unsuitable for assigning users to predefined profiles. Yang (n.d.) applies fuzzy K-means, achieving a silhouette coefficient of 0.690 and demonstrating that users can be segmented into distinct, well-separated financial behavioral profiles without crisp boundaries—relevant to Odin's design if profile boundaries are expected to be gradual rather than sharp. Duvalla (n.d.) reports that multi-dimensional behavioral segmentation using clustering and RNNs captures up to 15 distinct patterns, far exceeding demographic-only approaches, and that human-in-the-loop escalation for uncertain predictions improves model accuracy by 35%—a confidence-based design applicable to Odin's profile assignment at onboarding when transaction data is sparse. Zhou and He (n.d.) provide the algorithmic justification for multi-view integration (MUVIR) in rare category detection, relevant to profiling users based on heterogeneous features (income type, spending frequency, category preferences, cultural obligation signals).

For supervised classification specifically, Martínez et al. (n.d.) compare Random Forest, XGBoost, and SVM classifiers on high-dimensional imbalanced financial data, finding that ensemble methods (XGBoost, LightGBM, Random Forest) consistently achieve AUC-ROC of 0.90–0.93, outperforming deep neural networks on tabular data—a finding directly applicable to Odin's tabular feature space (income, demographics, initial spending patterns). Compagnino et al. (n.d.) note that supervised classifiers like Random Forest achieve high accuracy but may miss rare behavioral patterns (recall ≤ 0.36 on imbalanced data)—motivating cost-sensitive learning for underrepresented profile types. Dimaunahan et al. (n.d.) provide the feature selection foundation: income, occupation, monthly expenses, goal acceptance, and goal commitment are the strongest predictors of financial planning behavior among Filipino millennials—these should be the primary classification features at onboarding.

**Synthesis.** The profile classification algorithm literature supports a hybrid approach for Odin: supervised classification (XGBoost or Random Forest) for users with sufficient onboarding data, with fuzzy or confidence-weighted assignment for cold-start users who have minimal transaction history. The transition from initial profile assignment (based on demographics and self-reported financial goals) to behavior-updated profiles (based on accumulated transaction patterns) requires a dynamic reclassification mechanism that no single reviewed paper fully implements for the PFMS context—this is a design gap that Odin must address through its profile update logic. The methodological challenge of defining meaningful, discrete profile categories from continuous behavioral data, in a way that is both interpretable to users and actionable for the recommendation and anomaly detection modules, remains an open design problem. The reviewed literature provides algorithmic candidates (XGBoost, fuzzy K-means, RNN-based segmentation) but not a validated profile taxonomy for Filipino young professional users—another original contribution of Odin's development.

## G. Expense Categorization

### G.1 Expense Categorization in Filipino Personal Finance Contexts

Odin's expense categorization module classifies user-entered transactions into a structured taxonomy grounded in Philippine institutional spending frameworks. The literature reviewed here addresses both the technical approaches to categorization and the cultural and institutional requirements specific to the Filipino context.

On the technical side, machine learning approaches to expense categorization have achieved consistently high accuracy in non-Filipino contexts. Badiger et al. (n.d.) demonstrate that XGBoost achieves 91.3% weighted F1-score across 18 categories on a 4,200-transaction test set, reducing manual categorization effort by approximately 78%. Bashshar et al. (n.d.) show that Random Forest with TF-IDF achieves 86.2% accuracy on financial transaction descriptions, establishing a simpler but competitive baseline. Bhavana et al. (n.d.) report 91% F1-score using XGBoost and BERT for expense classification, while Jayaprakashnarayan et al. (n.d.) achieve 96.8% entity extraction accuracy using a multi-task transformer (MuRIL) on SMS transaction data in an on-device deployment. Chahar et al. (n.d.) present an AI-powered PFMS with a user-rated categorization accuracy of 4.2/5, using automated classification combined with manual review for ambiguous cases. Patel and Singh (n.d.) validate the hybrid rule-based plus ML approach, noting that automation reduces manual effort and that dynamic budget planning derived from historical categorized spending improves adherence. Yadav et al. (n.d.) add that OCR-based receipt scanning achieves 94% accuracy—a complementary input channel that Odin may consider for future versions.

The Philippine institutional grounding for Odin's category design is established through official data sources reviewed in the literature. BSP CFS (2021) confirms that the 2020 PCOICOP framework (shared between the FIES and CFS) is the authoritative standard for Filipino household expenditure categorization, with food at home (55.4% of expenditure) as the dominant category. Albert et al. (n.d.) and Ama (n.d.) provide FIES-based expenditure share data, with Ama (n.d.) further confirming sub-category breakdowns (bread 29.5%, meat 14.8%, fish 14.2% of food spending) that can calibrate Odin's default category weights. Bayangos and Lubangco (n.d.) provide validation from a different angle: remittance-receiving households spend directly on FIES categories (food, education, health, housing, durable goods, clothing), confirming that FIES categories map to real spending decisions. BSP CES (2026 Q1) provides the dynamic complement: real-time spending intention data across food, transport, and personal care categories, which can serve as contextual input to Odin's forecasting module.

The cultural specificity of Filipino expense categories is documented across multiple studies. Rosario (n.d.) shows that indigenous households treat kinship obligations (og-ogfo, ritual contributions) as "protected categories" that are non-negotiable even under scarcity—warranting dedicated expense categories rather than generic "miscellaneous" placement. Casilan and Baclagan (n.d.) and Casiño et al. (n.d.) confirm that family support contributions are a regular expenditure for Filipino young professionals, taking multiple forms (direct cash, payment of bills, groceries). Delos Santos et al. (n.d.) document that Filipino micro-entrepreneurs prefer informal saving methods (paluwagan, AWM 3.22 versus savings deposit AWM 3.22)—evidence that paluwagan contributions should have a dedicated category in Odin. Nduka and Benedicto (n.d.) identify the specific expense categories most burdensome for Filipino nurses: rent, utilities, food, dependents' schooling, and remittances—a category list that aligns with FIES structure but extends it with obligation-specific sub-categories. Efendi and Widagdo (n.d.) show that even informal financial actors naturally develop three-tier prioritization (primary needs, secondary needs, tertiary needs), suggesting that Odin's category structure should align with this cognitive hierarchy.

Cho (n.d.) adds a user behavior dimension: ethnic minority young adults (including Filipinos) in Hong Kong find budgeting "tedious, time-consuming, and stress-inducing," and the most common abandonment trigger is category ambiguity—when users are unsure which category a transaction belongs to. This motivates Odin's category design to minimize overlap, provide clear definitions, and support quick-entry defaults that reduce the cognitive load of classification.

**Synthesis.** The literature on expense categorization in Filipino personal finance contexts establishes a clear design framework for Odin's categorization module: a FIES/PCOICOP-aligned base taxonomy, extended with culturally specific categories (paluwagan, remittances, family support, SSS/Pag-IBIG/PhilHealth contributions), implemented through a hybrid rule-based and ML approach that accommodates manual transaction entry. The methodological diversity of the reviewed literature—from official national surveys (BSP CFS, FIES) to ML benchmarks (Badiger et al., Bashshar et al., Jayaprakashnarayan et al.) to qualitative cultural analyses (Rosario, Casilan & Baclagan, Efendi & Widagdo)—provides multi-layered justification for both the technical and cultural dimensions of Odin's categorization design.

A critical gap in this literature is the absence of expense categorization systems validated specifically on Filipino transaction descriptions in Taglish (mixed Tagalog-English), which is the primary language of informal financial communication among Filipino young professionals. All high-accuracy ML categorization systems reviewed (XGBoost, BERT, MuRIL) are trained on English or Indian language transaction data; their performance on Filipino-language transaction labels is unknown. Odin's categorization module will need to develop or adapt labeled training data in the Filipino linguistic context—an original contribution that the reviewed literature does not anticipate.

## H. Data Privacy, Security, and User Trust

### H.1 Data Privacy, Security, and User Trust in Personal Finance Systems

The privacy and security of personal financial data is a foundational design requirement for Odin, not an add-on compliance obligation. The literature reviewed here establishes both the technical standards and the user behavioral implications of privacy design in personal finance applications.

The sensitivity of personal financial data and the risks of mishandling it are well-documented. Whitaker (n.d.) and Ying and Blaise (n.d.) both note that algorithmic bias audits are necessary for ML-based financial decisions, and that data privacy violations in personal finance systems produce measurable harm to user trust and engagement. Compagnino et al. (n.d.) and George et al. (n.d.) establish from fraud detection literature that privacy-preserving techniques (federated learning, differential privacy) reduce the accuracy-privacy trade-off to approximately 5–15% in financial applications, suggesting that Odin can maintain meaningful privacy protection without severely compromising model performance. Balcazar-Paiva et al. (n.d.) identify from systematic review that privacy, fairness, and algorithmic bias are structural conditions for responsible AI deployment in personal finance systems—not peripheral concerns. Kalideen (n.d.) adds that explainable AI (SHAP, LIME) is essential for regulatory compliance and user trust in financial alert systems, and that federated learning enables collaborative model training without sharing raw transaction data—a design direction for Odin's future model improvement.

Technical security standards for financial application data handling are established across multiple reviewed papers. Arinze (n.d.) establishes that mobile-first databases must include end-to-end encryption, TLS for data in transit, and offline local storage with conflict resolution for reliable mobile financial app performance. Faisal et al. (n.d.) synthesize 112 studies showing that robust security measures (encryption, multi-factor authentication, fraud detection) and transparent data usage policies are critical for building and maintaining user trust in digital financial applications. Jayaprakashnarayan et al. (n.d.) provide the production-level security architecture: on-device 8-bit quantization combined with encrypted storage and differential privacy enables sophisticated ML inference with user data remaining local. Bhavana et al. (n.d.) specify that SHAP and LIME-based explanations improve user trust by translating anomaly scores into plain-English reasons, and that privacy safeguards (differential privacy, federated learning) are necessary for responsible personal finance AI deployment. Al-E'mari et al. (n.d.) confirm that GDPR-equivalent requirements (privacy-by-design, anonymization, encryption) apply to AI systems handling personal financial information—directly relevant to Odin's Philippine regulatory context under RA 10173.

The Philippine regulatory framework for personal financial data is addressed in contextually relevant studies. Velez (2025) notes that only 34% of low-income Filipino users understand digital payment security features—requiring that Odin embed plain-language security explanations and privacy controls rather than relying on user literacy. Bongado et al. (n.d.) find that perceived trust shows the widest variability (SD = 1.24) among digital wallet adoption factors among Filipino HEI students—underscoring that trust is not uniformly established and must be actively earned through transparent privacy design. Ashrafuzzaman et al. (n.d.) specify that emerging market deployments of AI personalization require consent dashboards, personalized privacy notices, and explainable AI prompts as necessary conditions for user trust.

The behavioral implications of privacy concern on transaction logging are addressed in several studies. Aoun et al. (n.d.) document that institutional trust erosion (as in Lebanon's financial crisis) amplifies the role of subjective attitudes over objective knowledge in shaping financial behavior—a finding that extends to app trust contexts. The AI-powered PFMS literature consistently finds that transparent, explainable systems achieve higher user engagement and data completeness: Badiger et al. (n.d.) report that Gemini LLM insights with RAG are rated useful by 84% of users when they can see how the system uses their data, and Hashemi et al. (n.d.) warn that unexplained algorithmic decisions reduce trust in 23% of users.

**Synthesis.** The data privacy, security, and user trust literature establishes a consistent and reinforcing set of design requirements for Odin: on-device data storage (or heavily encrypted cloud storage), end-to-end encryption for data in transit, privacy-by-design architecture that minimizes data collection to what is necessary for the system's functions, transparent privacy controls accessible to users with limited digital literacy, and explainable outputs for anomaly alerts and budget recommendations that do not inadvertently erode trust. The methodological range—from technical security benchmarks (Arinze, Jayaprakashnarayan et al.) to user trust studies (Faisal et al., Bongado et al.) to systematic reviews of AI ethics in finance (Balcazar-Paiva et al., Whitaker)—provides multi-layered justification for Odin's privacy-by-design approach.

A significant gap is the absence of research that has operationalized the Philippine Data Privacy Act (RA 10173) specifically for mobile PFMS applications in the local regulatory context. The reviewed literature references GDPR, NDPR, and equivalent frameworks but not RA 10173 or the National Privacy Commission's issuances relevant to personal finance applications. Odin's development must explicitly map its data handling architecture to RA 10173 requirements—an original compliance contribution that the reviewed international literature does not provide.

## I. User Retention and Engagement

### I.1 User Retention and Engagement in Personal Finance Systems

Odin's value depends entirely on users maintaining consistent transaction logging behavior. The literature on user retention and engagement in personal finance systems identifies the mechanisms that sustain engagement and the failure points that cause drop-off.

The relationship between data completeness and ML model performance is documented across multiple contexts. D'Souza et al. (n.d.) confirm that supervised ML models for expense categorization require complete labeled datasets, and that accuracy degrades for users with irregular income patterns—making engagement and data completeness a precondition for system intelligence. Chahar et al. (n.d.) and Badiger et al. (n.d.) both report that consistent transaction logging (even manual) enables the ML modules (forecasting, anomaly detection, categorization) to produce reliable outputs, validating the system's value proposition for users who log consistently. The implication is circular but empirically grounded: system value depends on engagement, and engagement depends on demonstrated system value—a virtuous cycle that Odin must engineer through its retention mechanisms.

Drop-off patterns and their causes are documented in the personal finance and mobile application literature. Jamal and Hashmat (n.d.) identify that rapid user expectation evolution, notification overload, and onboarding complexity are the primary causes of early abandonment in mobile applications. Cho (n.d.) finds that young adults abandon budgeting specifically because it is "tedious and stress-inducing"—with category ambiguity and entry friction as the dominant trigger. Imawan et al. (n.d.) show that 78% of users intend to continue using a basic tracking app after two weeks, but this measure was taken within an initial enthusiasm window and does not reflect long-term habit formation. The consistent implication across these studies is that Odin must design for sustained engagement beyond the honeymoon period through mechanisms that deliver value on every session.

Manual data entry is the primary friction point documented in personal finance research. Badiger et al. (n.d.) report a 78% reduction in manual logging effort through ML-based automated categorization—validating Odin's goal of reducing entry burden even within a manual-input model through smart defaults, category prediction, and quick-entry interfaces. D'Acunto and Rossi (n.d.) demonstrate that even simple balance sheet visibility (income aggregators) reduces spending and improves financial decisions—establishing that the mere act of logging and categorizing transactions generates value independent of the ML predictions. Chepkutwo (n.d.) argues that frugal fintech design with minimal required actions at each session achieves higher sustained engagement than feature-rich apps—arguing for Odin to minimize session interaction requirements to the essential logging act.

Non-gamification retention mechanisms are specifically supported by the literature. Imawan et al. (n.d.) find that visual goal progress bars and automated reminders are the most effective features for maintaining consistent tracking behavior, without requiring gamification. Faisal et al. (n.d.) synthesize evidence that AI-driven personalization improves 90-day retention by 19 percentage points versus non-personalized services—directly supporting Odin's intelligent, behavior-responsive modules as the primary retention mechanism. D'Acunto and Rossi (n.d.) confirm that robo-advisor features (goal-setting, overspending alerts, peer comparison) each produce non-reverting reductions in spending and increases in saving, establishing demonstrated value as the sustainable retention driver. Ashrafuzzaman et al. (n.d.) add that predictive churn analysis using behavior sequences is a viable non-gamification alternative to extrinsic rewards for retention. Casalhay et al. (n.d.) and Aoun et al. (n.d.) both confirm that Filipino young professionals already seek financial guidance and budget tools voluntarily—establishing intrinsic motivation as a realistic foundation for engagement.

The minimum viable engagement frequency is addressed indirectly in several studies. Imawan et al. (n.d.) suggest that two weeks of consistent logging is sufficient for users to perceive value from visual progress and reminders, though long-term habit formation is not measured. Dewi (n.d.) finds that gamification elements build discipline and reduce high-cost loan reliance—but Odin's non-gamification design requires alternative value demonstrations at the same frequency. The BSP CES (2026 Q1) finding that Filipino consumer spending intentions change measurably quarter-over-quarter suggests that monthly engagement (aligned with Odin's forecasting cycle) may be the natural minimum for maintaining perceived relevance.

**Synthesis.** The user retention and engagement literature converges on a set of evidence-based design principles for Odin: minimize entry friction at every session, deliver demonstrated value (personalized forecasts, anomaly alerts, goal progress) on a consistent schedule, use smart notifications to maintain engagement without generating notification fatigue, and prioritize intrinsic value delivery over extrinsic gamification incentives. The methodological range across the reviewed studies—from systematic reviews of mobile finance adoption (Faisal et al., Ashrafuzzaman et al.) to usability evaluations of personal finance apps (Imawan et al., Jamal & Hashmat) to behavioral economics evidence on tool-mediated financial improvement (D'Acunto & Rossi, Badiger et al.) to Filipino-context studies (Casalhay et al., Aoun et al.)—provides converging multi-disciplinary justification for this design orientation.

The primary gap in this literature is the absence of longitudinal studies measuring engagement and retention in AI-powered PFMS applications—particularly in Philippine or similar Southeast Asian contexts. The available user studies measure engagement over days or weeks (within initial enthusiasm windows) but do not track whether consistent engagement is maintained over the months required for Odin's ML modules to accumulate enough data to deliver reliable forecasts and anomaly detection. This constitutes both a key design challenge and an original empirical contribution that Odin's longitudinal evaluation can address.

## J. System Evaluation

### J.1 System Evaluation in Personal Finance and Budget Management Systems

Odin's evaluation plan employs a combined ISO/IEC 25010:2023 quality assessment and System Usability Scale (SUS) administration to provide a comprehensive, standards-compliant evaluation framework. The literature reviewed here establishes the theoretical basis, methodological guidance, and applied precedents for this combined approach.

The case for ISO/IEC 25010 as a software quality evaluation framework is established in the literature through its application to comparable intelligent systems. Al-E'mari et al. (n.d.) apply a KPI framework aligned with ISO 25010 quality characteristics (time savings, error reduction, accuracy) to evaluate AI-driven financial decision systems, validating the framework's applicability to intelligent financial tools. Bhavana et al. (n.d.) conduct a 100-user pilot evaluation that implicitly maps to ISO 25010 characteristics including functional suitability (anomaly detection accuracy), performance efficiency (response time), and usability (SUS score mentioned, though not reported). Boniol et al. (n.d.) provide the recommendation that Volume Under the Surface (VUS-ROC) is the preferred evaluation measure for time-series anomaly detection due to its robustness to label noise, while MAPE, RMSE, and MAE are established as the standard metrics for forecasting performance (Kara & Şengüler). Martínez et al. (n.d.) establish that AUC-PR (not AUC-ROC) is the preferred metric for imbalanced financial datasets where anomalous events are rare. Pagliaro (n.d.) establishes the methodological standard for time-series model evaluation: walk-forward validation rather than k-fold cross-validation, and multi-model comparison as the most credible evidence design. These collectively establish the module-level evaluation protocol within Odin's ISO 25010 framework.

The System Usability Scale is validated for mobile application evaluation in multiple reviewed studies. Imawan et al. (n.d.) evaluate a mobile PFM app achieving 4.6/5 (Excellent) usability with 50 young adult respondents, providing a direct benchmark for Odin's SUS-equivalent evaluation. Chahar et al. (n.d.) report a user satisfaction score of 4.4/5 and ease of use of 4.5/5 from a comparable prototype evaluation with no specified sample size. Yadav et al. (n.d.) conduct a 50-user evaluation using 5-point Likert ratings for perceived relevance and empathy, establishing a lightweight but precedent-setting evaluation template for early-stage PFMS. Jayaprakashnarayan et al. (n.d.) report System Usability Scale intent and specific mobile performance metrics (latency: 43–127ms, battery drain: 0.9–2.0%/hour), providing concrete mobile deployment benchmarks for Odin's ISO 25010 performance efficiency assessment. Badiger et al. (n.d.) report that 84% of users find Gemini-generated insights useful, providing a content relevance benchmark for Odin's recommendation module evaluation.

The combination of ISO 25010 and SUS as a joint evaluation framework is supported by the reviewed evidence on their complementary coverage. ISO 25010 provides multi-characteristic quality coverage (functional suitability, performance efficiency, compatibility, usability, reliability, security, maintainability, portability), while SUS captures the holistic user-perceived usability that multi-characteristic frameworks can miss when evaluated in isolation. Hashemi et al. (n.d.) explicitly demonstrate that explanation mechanisms require measurement of trust impact—a dimension that neither ISO 25010 nor SUS directly captures in standard administration, suggesting that Odin's evaluation protocol should include supplementary trust and explanation quality items.

Acknowledged limitations of both evaluation instruments inform Odin's evaluation design. SUS relies on post-task self-report and is insensitive to specific usability dimensions relevant to ML-driven features (e.g., perceived accuracy of forecasts, trust in anomaly alerts). ISO 25010 quality characteristics may not adequately capture the adaptive or personalized nature of Odin's intelligent modules. Pagliaro (n.d.) warns that 60–80% of published predictive anomalies fail replication under multiple testing corrections, arguing for out-of-sample validation across different user cohorts and time periods. Boniol et al. (n.d.) note that no single anomaly detection method outperforms all others across datasets—arguing for comparative evaluation using multiple methods and metrics. These limitations collectively motivate Odin's evaluation plan to complement ISO 25010 and SUS with module-level algorithm performance metrics (MAPE, AUC-PR, F1-score) and behavioral outcome measures (budget adherence rate, logging consistency).

Sample size and respondent profile guidelines are addressed in the applied literature. Yadav et al. (n.d.), Imawan et al. (n.d.), and Bhavana et al. (n.d.) all conduct evaluations with 50 users—establishing this as a practical and precedent-supported sample size for early-stage PFMS evaluation. Chahar et al. (n.d.) conduct a comparable evaluation with an unspecified but apparently similar sample, while Jayaprakashnarayan et al. (n.d.) focus their evaluation on system performance metrics rather than user sample size. The convergent precedent across these studies supports Odin's planned evaluation with a user sample in the 50-respondent range, specifically recruited from the target population of Filipino young professionals.

**Synthesis.** The system evaluation literature provides a well-established methodological framework for Odin's assessment: ISO/IEC 25010 for multi-characteristic software quality evaluation, SUS for holistic user-perceived usability, and module-level algorithm performance metrics (MAPE, AUC-PR, F1, VUS-ROC) for intelligent component validation. The applied precedents from comparable PFMS evaluations (Imawan et al., Chahar et al., Yadav et al.) establish that 50-user samples, 5-point rating instruments, and post-task self-report protocols are appropriate for early-stage personal finance system evaluation. The methodological warnings from Pagliaro (n.d.) and Boniol et al. (n.d.) on replication failure and single-method bias inform Odin's decision to use out-of-sample walk-forward validation for forecasting and comparative multi-metric evaluation for anomaly detection.

A gap in this literature is the absence of evaluation studies that apply ISO 25010 and SUS jointly to a PFMS with multiple intelligent ML modules in a Philippine mobile deployment context. Existing evaluations either assess usability alone (Imawan et al.) or algorithm performance alone (Bhavana et al., Martínez et al.), without integrating both into a single coherent evaluation framework. Odin's evaluation plan—combining ISO 25010 quality characteristics, SUS, and module-level algorithm benchmarks within a single study and a locally recruited Filipino respondent sample—represents an original methodological contribution that addresses this gap directly.
