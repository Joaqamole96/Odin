# A qualitative investigation of financial decisionmaking and enabling factors among ethnic minority young adults in Hong Kong

## TL;DR (One Sentence)
Ethnic minority young adults in Hong Kong save ~30% of income, use informal borrowing, and rely on family/peer support to navigate budgeting, spending, and fraud detection.

## Core Problem & Motivation
Existing financial literacy research focuses on Western populations, older adults, and quantitative methods. Day‑to‑day financial decisions (budgeting, saving, fraud detection) of younger ethnic minorities are underexamined, especially in non‑Western contexts like Hong Kong.

## Methods / Approach (Bulleted)
- **Qualitative design** – semi‑structured individual interviews.
- **53 participants** aged 18–29: Pakistani (16), Indian (13), Nepalese (13), Filipino (11).
- **Recruitment** via NGOs and university international offices (purposive sampling).
- **Data analysis** – thematic analysis (Braun & Clarke, 2006).
- **Interview focus** – budgeting, spending, financial planning, use of products, debt, fraud detection.

## Key Findings / Results (Numbered)
1. **Budgeting** – Most use saving‑centric or spending‑centric approaches; digital apps and parental monitoring common. Challenges: low income, high living costs, self‑control.
2. **Spending** – Modest, prioritizing food/transport. Strategies: deferred purchase, bargain shopping, lump‑sum payments. Philosophies: needs vs wants, YOLO, work‑as‑incentive.
3. **Saving** – Over half save **≥30% of monthly income**. Goals: education, property, business, retirement, financial autonomy.
4. **Financial products** – Insurance (most common), stocks, crypto, property/gold in home countries. Parental/peer support and self‑learning drive adoption.
5. **Debt** – Informal borrowing from family/friends is common; one‑third own credit cards (mostly safe but some pay only minimum due). Borrowing seen as shameful.
6. **Fraud detection** – Participants fall victim despite tactics (ignoring calls, pretending not to speak Cantonese). Vulnerable to charity, medical, online, and investment scams.
7. **Enabling factors** – Family social capital, intrapersonal characteristics (self‑discipline, future orientation), peer support, knowledge proficiency, real‑life lessons (employment, pandemic).

## Important Figures & Tables (Condensed)
- **Table 1 (participant characteristics)** – Demographics: 30 male, 23 female; 32 employed, 21 students; most with bachelor’s degree → *Sample is relatively well‑educated, limiting generalization to lower‑SES minorities.*
- **Table 2 (major themes)** – Maps practices and perceptions across six decision areas to financial knowledge, attitudes, behavior (KAB) → *Framework shows budgeting and spending are attitude‑driven, while product use and fraud detection rely on knowledge.*

| MPF | Mandatory Provident Fund – Hong Kong’s compulsory pension scheme |

| Mental accounting | Cognitive bias treating money differently based on source/purpose (e.g., credit card vs cash) |

## Critical Citations
- [Lusardi & Mitchell, 2011b] – Foundational work on financial literacy gaps in racial/ethnic minorities.
- [Atkinson & Messy, 2012] – Defines financial literacy as knowledge + skills + attitudes + behavior.
- [Thaler, 1985] – Mental accounting theory, used here to explain suboptimal credit card and saving choices.

## Relevance to Odin

**Topic(s):**
Topic 1: Spending and Budgeting Behavior of Filipino Young Professionals; Topic 4: Budgeting Strategies and Budget Recommendation in Personal Finance Systems; Topic 8: Expense Categorization in Filipino Personal Finance Contexts

**How it contributes:**
This paper provides rich qualitative evidence on how young adults actually budget (saving‑centric vs spending‑centric), use mental accounting, and leverage social support (family, peers) for financial decisions. These behavioral patterns directly inform Odin’s user profiling and budget recommendation modules, especially the need to support multiple budgeting philosophies and social accountability features.

**Directly justifies:**
- The design of dual budgeting modes (“save first” vs “spend first”) in Odin’s budget recommendation engine.
- Inclusion of social restraint mechanisms (e.g., shared savings goals or peer monitoring) as engagement features.
- Use of anti‑fraud education and scam pattern alerts within the app, based on participants’ demonstrated vulnerability.
- Incorporation of mental accounting nudges (e.g., showing annual interest cost of minimum credit card payments).

**Limitations of relevance:**
- Study population is Hong Kong ethnic minorities, not Filipino young professionals – cultural and regulatory contexts differ.
- Qualitative findings are not directly quantifiable for predictive modeling (Topic 5).
- No direct evaluation of mobile‑first design or digital tool effectiveness (Topic 3).

## Limitations & Assumptions (from paper)
- Sample is relatively well‑educated (half with bachelor’s degree) – may not represent lower‑SES ethnic minorities.
- Interviews in English exclude those with limited English proficiency.
- Small qualitative sample (n=53) prevents statistical generalization.
- No comparison group (ethnic Chinese young adults), so unique minority behaviors may be overstated.

## Takeaway for Memory (≤ 3 bullet points)
- 🔑 **Save 30% or more** – Most participants saved at least one‑third of income, using parental or peer “forced” savings.
- 💡 **Two budgeting styles** – “Save first, spend rest” vs “Spend limit, save rest” – both need app support.
- 📌 **Borrowing is shameful but common** – Informal loans from family/friends dominate; fraud awareness is low despite high scam exposure.
