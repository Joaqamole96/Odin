# Wealth Creation for Expanding the Middle Class in the Philippines

## TL;DR (One Sentence)
Philippine middle class grew to 39.8% of population in 2021 but COVID-19 erased recent gains, requiring four policy pathways for expansion.

## Core Problem & Motivation
The Philippines aims to become a predominantly middle-class society with zero poverty by 2040 (Ambisyon 2040). However, global headwinds (inflation, climate change, digital disruption) threaten this goal. Growing the middle class depends on *new wealth creation* across society, not just redistribution or poverty reduction.

## Methods / Approach (Bulleted)
- **Income-based definition**: middle class = households with per capita income between **2× and 12×** the official poverty line (varies by province/urban-rural).
- **Data sources**: 2021 Family Income and Expenditure Survey (FIES) and July 2021 Labor Force Survey (LFS), plus earlier FIES rounds (1991, 2006, 2015, 2018).
- **Seven income clusters** grouped into three broad classes: low-income (poor + near-poor), middle-income (lower, middle, upper middle), high-income (upper but not rich + rich).
- **Indicative monthly family income** for a family of 5 in 2021: PHP 24,060 – PHP 144,360 for middle class.

## Key Findings / Results (Numbered or Bulleted)
1. **Size & growth**: Middle class share rose from **28.5%** (1991) to 39.8% (2021) – but fell from **43.5%** in 2018 due to COVID-19.
2. **Urban concentration**: 60.6% of urban residents are middle class vs. only 33.8% in rural areas.
3. **Regional hotspots**: NCR (62% middle class), CAR (50.9%), CALABARZON (49.5%) – these three regions contribute 51.8% of all middle‑income Filipinos.
4. **Education**: 40.6% of middle class completed college vs. 14.5% of low-income class.
5. **Employment**: 79.5% have permanent jobs (vs. 31.4% low‑income have short‑term/casual).
6. **Asset ownership**: 98% own mobile phones (urban), 93% TV, 46% computer.
7. **Remittances matter**: 74.7% of OFW families are middle class; overseas remittances account for 8.3% of middle‑class income.
8. **Vulnerability**: The pandemic reversed gains – lower‑middle income segment most at risk of falling back into poverty.

## Equations to Remember
$$ \text{Middle-class threshold (monthly family income, 5 members)} = \$24{,}060 \text{ to } \$144{,}360 \text{ (PHP)} $$
*Any household earning between 2× and 12× the poverty line (PHP 12,030 per month for a family of 5) qualifies as middle class.*

## Critical Citations
- **Easterly (2001)** – Established the "middle-class consensus": larger middle class leads to better institutions and long-term growth.
- **Banerjee & Duflo (2008)** – Showed middle class in developing countries drives entrepreneurship and human capital investment.
- **Albert et al. (2018a)** – Provided the income‑based definition (2×–12× poverty line) used throughout this paper.

## Limitations & Assumptions
- **Income‑only definition** ignores education, occupation, and social capital (multidimensional nature of class).
- **Per capita income** does not account for economies of scale within households (e.g., shared housing costs).
- **Cross‑sectional data** (2021 FIES/LFS) captures a pandemic snapshot – may overstate vulnerability.
- **Regional poverty lines** vary but thresholds are fixed multiples – may not reflect local middle‑class norms.

## Takeaway for Memory (≤ 3 bullet points)
- 🔑 **2× to 12× the poverty line** – that’s the rule: earn between PHP 24k and PHP 144k monthly for a family of 5 to be middle class.
- 📉 **Pandemic erased 3.7 percentage points** – middle class share fell from 43.5% (2018) to 39.8% (2021), pushing millions back into low‑income.
- 🧠 **Four levers for wealth creation**: 1) Climate justice & natural resources, 2) Trade/MSME support, 3) Future‑ready workforce & social protection, 4) Digital governance.
