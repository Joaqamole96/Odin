# Beyond Awareness: Examining Financial Behaviors Among Public School Teachers in the Philippines

## TL;DR (One Sentence)
Philippine public school teachers show moderately positive financial behaviors driven by necessity, not mastery, and seminars have no effect.

## Core Problem & Motivation
Most financial literacy studies focus on what people know, not what they actually do. Teachers face a “knowledge‑action gap” – they know how to budget but struggle due to low income and high debt.

## Methods / Approach (Bulleted)
- Explanatory sequential mixed‑methods (survey + interviews).
- 335 regular public school teachers in Baguio City, Philippines.
- Survey measured financial behavior frequency (4‑point scale) and perceived difficulty.
- Semi‑structured interviews (9 teachers) explained quantitative patterns.
- Statistics: ANOVA, t‑tests, Friedman test, Nemenyi post hoc.

## Key Findings / Results (Numbered or Bulleted)
1. General financial behavior is **moderately positive** (mean = 2.69/4.00).
2. **Marital status matters**: Single teachers outperform married ones (p = .034).
3. **Employment rank matters**: Master teachers score higher (mean 3.00 vs 2.65, p = .002).
4. **Seminars have zero effect** – attendees and non‑attendees have identical mean (2.69, p = .991).
5. **Budgeting is hardest** (perceived difficulty 2.17/5), but performed often (endurance, not mastery).
6. **Choosing financial products is perceived as very easy** (4.15/5) despite low ownership, suggesting overconfidence (Dunning‑Kruger effect).

## Important Figures & Tables (Condensed)
- Table 1 (behavior domains): “Making ends meet” highest (2.90), “active saving” lowest (2.43).
- Table 6 (perceived difficulty): Budgeting = moderately difficult (2.17); choosing products = very easy (4.15).
- Key paradox: Budgeting is done often but feels hardest → *like running on a treadmill – you keep moving but get nowhere.*

## Equations to Remember
None. This is a behavioral study without mathematical models.

## Definitions & Acronyms
| Term/Acronym | Definition |
|--------------|------------|
| **Conscious constraint** | Disciplined financial management under limited income, without real flexibility (the study’s core concept). |
| **OECD/INFE** | Organisation for Economic Co‑operation and Development – International Network on Financial Education. |
| **GSIS/Pag‑IBIG** | Mandatory government savings and loan programs for Philippine public employees. |

## Critical Citations
- [Reyiso‑Cruz, 2019] – Documents high teacher debt in the Philippines (P319B).
- [Kaiser & Menkhoff, 2017] – Shows one‑off financial seminars have little behavioral impact.
- [Lusardi & Mitchell, 2014] – Foundational work on financial literacy and economic outcomes.

## Limitations & Assumptions
- Single city (Baguio) – may not generalize to rural areas.
- Self‑reported behaviors – possible social desirability bias.
- Cross‑sectional design – cannot prove causation (e.g., rank → behavior).
- No objective measure of income or debt levels.

## Takeaway for Memory (≤ 3 bullet points)
- 🔑 **Seminars change nothing** – attendees and non‑attendants score identically (2.69). *Awareness without structural support is useless.*
- 💡 **Budgeting feels hardest** because teachers have “nothing left to save” – *conscious constraint* means you manage scarcity, not wealth.
- 📌 **Marriage and rank matter** – Married and non‑master teachers need targeted debt and family‑based interventions, not generic advice.
