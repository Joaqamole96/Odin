# Impact of financial literacy, mental budgeting and self control on financial wellbeing: Mediating impact of investment decision making

## TL;DR (One Sentence)
Financial literacy, mental budgeting, and self‑control boost financial wellbeing, partly through better investment decisions.

## Core Problem & Motivation
Financial distress harms mental health and productivity, yet the combined effect of cognitive factors (financial literacy, mental budgeting, self‑control) on subjective financial wellbeing remains unclear. This study tests whether investment decision‑making mediates these relationships.

## Methods / Approach (Bulleted)
- **Sample:** 449 Chinese university students (55% male, 60% business majors).
- **Analysis:** Partial Least Squares Structural Equation Modeling (PLS‑SEM).
- **Measures:** Validated scales for financial wellbeing (7 items), financial literacy (7 items), mental budgeting (4 items), self‑control (5+4 items), and investment decision behavior (4 components).
- **Data collection:** Convenience sampling via physical and electronic channels.

## Key Findings / Results (Numbered)
1. Financial literacy has a **direct positive effect** on financial wellbeing (β = 0.299, p < 0.001).
2. Mental budgeting directly improves financial wellbeing (β = 0.102, p < 0.001).
3. Self‑control directly enhances financial wellbeing (β = 0.182, p < 0.001).
4. Investment decision making **partially mediates** all three relationships (indirect effects: FL: 0.017, MB: 0.016, SC: 0.019; all p < 0.05).
5. All three cognitive factors also positively influence investment decision making (β: 0.136 to 0.152, p < 0.001).

## Definitions & Acronyms
| Term/Acronym | Definition |
|--------------|------------|
| FL | Financial literacy – knowledge and skills to manage money effectively. |
| MB | Mental budgeting – cognitive tracking and categorizing of expenses/income. |
| SC | Self‑control – ability to resist impulses and stick to long‑term financial plans. |
| DMB | Investment decision‑making behavior – choices about savings, investments, and credit. |
| FWB | Financial wellbeing – subjective evaluation of one's financial situation. |
| PLS‑SEM | Partial Least Squares Structural Equation Modeling – a statistical method for path analysis. |

## Critical Citations
- [Taft et al., 2013] – First established direct link between financial literacy and FWB.
- [Chun & Johnson, 2021] – Showed mental budgeting reduces overspending (like a mental "spending diary").
- [Thaler & Shefrin, 1981] – Foundational economic theory of self‑control (planner vs. doer model).

## Limitations & Assumptions
- **Sample:** Only Chinese university students – may not generalize to older or less educated populations.
- **Cross‑sectional design:** Cannot prove causality (only associations).
- **Self‑reported measures:** Risk of social desirability bias.
- **Linear relationships assumed:** Possible non‑linear effects (e.g., extreme financial stress) not tested.

## Takeaway for Memory (≤ 3 bullet points)
- 🔑 **FL strongest direct boost** – each 1‑point increase in financial literacy raises FWB by 0.30 (largest effect).
- 💡 **Partial mediation matters** – investment decisions explain about 5‑10% of the total effect; the rest is direct.
- 📌 **Three pillars** – financial literacy + mental budgeting + self‑control work together; strengthen all three for best wellbeing.
