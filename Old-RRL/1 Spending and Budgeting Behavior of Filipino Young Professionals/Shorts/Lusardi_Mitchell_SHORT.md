# The Importance of Financial Literacy: Opening a New Field

## TL;DR (One Sentence)
Only 43% of Americans understand basic financial concepts, and financial literacy explains 30–40% of wealth inequality near retirement.

## Core Problem & Motivation
People face complex lifelong financial decisions (loans, retirement, inflation) but lack basic knowledge. Financial illiteracy contributes to wealth inequality and macroeconomic fragility.

## Methods / Approach (Bulleted)
- Designed “Big Three” survey questions (interest, inflation, risk diversification) for the Health and Retirement Study (2004).
- Fielded questions in the 2019 Survey of Consumer Finances (SCF) and 40+ international surveys.
- Used median regressions to link financial literacy to wealth outcomes.
- Built a theoretical life‑cycle model where literacy is an endogenous investment (costs vs. benefits, depreciation over time).

## Key Findings / Results (Numbered or Bulleted)
1. **Only 43%** of Americans answer all Big Three correctly.
2. Gender gap: 29% of women vs. 48% of men get all three right; women more often say “do not know.”
3. Financial literacy follows a hump‑shaped age pattern, peaking at ages 50–59 (≈50% correct).
4. Education gap: 65% of college graduates score perfect vs. 18% of high‑school dropouts.
5. Race/ethnicity gap: 50% of Whites perfect vs. 26% of Blacks and 22% of Hispanics.
6. One additional correct answer on the FinLit index is associated with **13% higher median net wealth** (Table 3).
7. Financial literacy accounts for **30–40% of wealth inequality** near retirement.
8. Meta‑analysis of 76 RCTs shows financial education improves both knowledge and behavior (effect 3–5× larger than older studies).

## Critical Citations
- [Lusardi & Mitchell, 2011a] – Introduced the Big Three and showed strong link to retirement planning.
- [Lusardi, Michaud, & Mitchell, 2017] – Developed the endogenous literacy model and quantified literacy’s role in wealth inequality (30–40%).
- [Kaiser et al., 2022] – Meta‑analysis of 76 RCTs proving financial education works (cost‑effective, similar to health/energy programs).

## Limitations & Assumptions
- Cross‑sectional data cannot fully separate age vs. cohort effects (though longitudinal study confirms age decline).
- Financial literacy is endogenous (people may invest in literacy because they are already wealthy) – causal claims rely on theoretical model and RCT evidence.
- Big Three are simplistic; may miss context‑specific knowledge.
- SCF data (2019) pre‑dates recent high inflation; pandemic effects were studied separately.

## Takeaway for Memory (≤ 3 bullet points)
- 📊 **Only 4 in 10 Americans pass basic financial literacy** – and literacy drives **30–40% of wealth inequality** at retirement.
- 🧠 **Women and minorities lag most** (e.g., women: 29% correct vs. 48% men) – partly due to lower confidence (“do not know” responses).
- 💰 **Every correct answer on the FinLit test = +13% more net wealth** – financial education in schools/workplace works and is cost‑effective.
