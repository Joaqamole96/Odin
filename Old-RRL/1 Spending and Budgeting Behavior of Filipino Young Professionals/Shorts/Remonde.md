# THE EFFECTIVENESS OF FINANCIAL LITERACY PROGRAM ON FINANCIAL MANAGEMENT SKILLS OF MILLENNIAL TEACHERS

## TL;DR (One Sentence)
A 10‑area financial literacy program moved millennial teachers from “Approaching Proficient” (mean 32.22) to “Advanced” (mean 43.19) in financial management skills.

## Core Problem & Motivation
Millennial teachers in Digos City lacked basic financial knowledge, leading to poor budgeting, debt, investing, and emergency fund practices. No prior tailored intervention existed for this group.

## Methods / Approach (Bulleted)
- **Design:** Descriptive pre‑experimental (one‑group pre‑test / post‑test)
- **Sample:** 36 millennial teachers (born early 1980s–mid‑1990s, ≥2 years teaching experience)
- **Intervention:** Financial Literacy Program with 10 modules (savings, budgeting, investing, debt, emergency funds, insurance, loans, expenditures, tax, retirement)
- **Instrument:** 47‑item multiple‑choice survey validated by financial experts
- **Analysis:** Paired Samples t‑test (α = 0.05)

## Key Findings / Results (Numbered or Bulleted)
1. Pre‑test overall mean = **32.22** (Approaching Proficient); emergency funds highest (3.53), investing lowest (3.14).
2. Post‑test overall mean = **43.19** (Advanced) – all ten areas reached “Advance” level.
3. Largest gain: debt management (from 2.89 → 4.00). Smallest gain: investing (3.14 → 4.28).
4. Paired t‑test: significant difference (p = .000) – reject null hypothesis.
5. **34% increase** in overall raw score (32.22 → 43.19 out of 47).

## Important Figures & Tables (Condensed)
- **Table 1 (Pre‑test):** All skills “Approaching Proficient” or “Developing” → baseline weak in investing (3.14) & budgeting (3.31).
- **Table 2 (Post‑test):** All skills “Advance” – mean range 3.69 (insurance) to 4.75 (retirement planning).
- **Table 3 (t‑test):** Before = 32.33; After = 43.19; Sig. = .000.

## Critical Citations
- **Lusardi & Mitchell (2014)** – Foundational theory that financial literacy programs transform financial decision‑making.
- **Miraj et al. (2023)** – Showed financial literacy programs improve budgeting, risk management, investing in Pakistani millennials.
- **Fernandes et al. (2014)** – Meta‑evidence that customized financial education improves downstream financial behaviors.

## Limitations & Assumptions
- No control group (pre‑experimental design) – causality limited.
- Small sample (n=36) from one city (Digos) – not generalizable to all Filipino teachers.
- Self‑reported data – possible social desirability bias.
- Short‑term measurement – no follow‑up to test retention.

## Takeaway for Memory (≤ 3 bullet points)
- 🔑 **+10.97 point jump** – program transformed “Approaching” into “Advanced” in all ten financial skills.
- 💡 **Weakest area (investing) grew most** – from Developing (3.14) to Advanced (4.28) – *like learning to ride a bike with training wheels then without*.
- 📌 **Debt management skyrocketed** – lowest pre‑test score (2.89) became one of the highest post‑test (4.00) – shows targeted teaching works.
